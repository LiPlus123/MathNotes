## 4 First-Order Logic

The goal of the logicist program, initiated by Gottlob Frege in the second half of the nineteenth century and pursued by Bertrand Russell and Alfred North Whitehead in the early twentieth century, was to reduce all or at least substantial parts of mathematics to pure logic. The common view today is that the program has failed, since all the foundations for mathematics that have been proposed require assumptions that do not seem to have a purely logical character. Thus the common view is that mathematics is best understood as the result of logical reasoning from properly mathematical axioms.

There is not, however, full agreement as to where to draw the line between logic and mathematics. Philosophers since Gottfried Leibniz have characterized logic as a means of determining necessary truths, that is, those assertions that are true in all possible worlds. George Boole and Frege characterized logic as a codification of the fundamental laws of thought. In the early twentieth century, the logical positivist movement tried to characterize the principles of logic as a matter of linguistic convention. Others, like Russell, have characterized logic as the general principles of reasoning that can be applied to any domain of inquiry. With this in mind, some have maintained that logic should be ontologically neutral: if its laws are supposed to hold in any possible domain, they should hold in domains that are empty, and so no logical principle should presuppose or imply the existence of objects (other than, perhaps, purely logical ones).

Whatever the extent of logical reasoning may be, first-order logic fares well on most accounts, since it describes constructs like "and," "every," "some," and "if ... then," which play a fundamental role in our language and thought. Our deductive systems for first-order logic will assume that the intended domain is nonempty, but they are nonetheless sound for domains with just a single element, showing that the logic itself does not presuppose a strong ontology.

The differences between classical and intuitionistic logic reflect different understandings of the nature of logic reasoning. What these differences come to in practical terms will be clarified as we continue to study these two versions of logic and their formal properties.

### 4.1 The Language of First-Order Logic

Remember that a first-order language, \( L \) , is a tuple \( \left( {\Gamma ,\Delta , a}\right) \) in which \( \Gamma \) is the set of function symbols, \( \Delta \) is the set of relation symbols, and \( a \) is a function that assigns an arity to each element of \( \Gamma  \cup  \Delta \) . In contrast to propositional logic, the expressions of first-order logic are defined in two stages: first we define the set of terms, and then we define the set of formulas. The set of terms of \( L \) has already been defined in Section 1.3 inductively as follows:

- Each variable \( x \) is a term.

- If \( c \in  \Gamma \) is a constant symbol (a 0-ary function symbol), then \( c \) is a term.

- If \( f \in  \Gamma \) is a \( k \) -ary function symbol with \( k > 0 \) and \( {t}_{0},\ldots ,{t}_{k - 1} \) are all terms, then so is \( f\left( {{t}_{0},\ldots ,{t}_{k - 1}}\right) \) .

The set of terms is freely generated, so we have principles of induction and recursion on terms. In particular, we can define functions like variables \( \left( t\right) \) , depth \( \left( t\right) \) , and the substitution operation \( t\left\lbrack  {s/x}\right\rbrack \) as in Section 1.3, and we can prove that they have the expected properties.

We define the set of first-order formulas of \( L \) as described in Section 1.6, identifying formulas up to renaming of their free variables. Thus the set of formulas is generated inductively by the following clauses:

- \( \bot \) is a formula.

- If \( s \) and \( t \) are terms, \( s = t \) is a formula.

- If \( {t}_{0},\ldots ,{t}_{k - 1} \) are terms and \( R \) is a \( k \) -ary relation symbol of \( L \) , then \( R\left( {{t}_{0},\ldots ,{t}_{k - 1}}\right) \) is a formula.

- If \( A \) and \( B \) are formulas, then so are \( \left( {A \land  B}\right) ,\left( {A \vee  B}\right) \) , and \( \left( {A \rightarrow  B}\right) \) .

- If \( A \) is a formula and \( x \) is a variable, then \( \left( {\forall {xA}}\right) \) and \( \left( {\exists {xA}}\right) \) are formulas.

The set of formulas is not freely generated by these conditions: if \( y \) is not free in \( A \) , then \( \forall {xA} \) and \( \forall {yA}\left\lbrack  {y/x}\right\rbrack \) are considered the same. We can nonetheless define functions by recursion on the complexity of \( A \) , as long as we specify the value at \( \forall {xA} \) in terms of the value at \( A \) in such a way that the same value is assigned if we consider \( A\left\lbrack  {y/x}\right\rbrack \) instead, for any \( y \) not free in \( A \) .

In the definition above, the equality symbol is used with infix notation, but otherwise it behaves just like a binary relation symbol in the language. We will, however, continue to give it special status as one of the basic logical symbols. It is sometimes useful to consider first-order logic without the equality symbol, in which case I will refer to first-order logic without equality. By default, first-order logic is first-order logic with equality.

Formulas of the form \( s = t, \bot \) , and \( R\left( {{t}_{0},\ldots ,{t}_{k - 1}}\right) \) are said to be atomic. These constitute the base cases in proofs that proceed by induction on formulas. As was the case with terms, a formula with free variables is only meaningful relative to an assignment to these free variables. A formula with no free variables is called a sentence.

In the metatheory, we have already begun to use \( x, y, z,\ldots \) to range over variables, \( r, s \) , \( t,\ldots \) to range over terms, and \( A, B, C,\ldots \) to range over formulas. The conventions for dropping parentheses carry over from propositional logic, with the added stipulation that ∀ and \( \exists \) bind tightest of all, and are read from right to left. So, for example, the formula

\[
\forall {xA} \land  \exists {yB} \rightarrow  C
\]

is understood as

\[
\left( {\left( {\left( {\forall {xA}}\right)  \land  \left( {\exists {yB}}\right) }\right)  \rightarrow  C}\right) \text{ . }
\]

Be warned that conventions vary. I have already noted in the preface that in computer science the convention is to give universal and existential quantifiers the widest scope possible, so that \( \forall {xA} \land  B \) abbreviates \( \forall x\left( {A \land  B}\right) \) .

Since 0-ary relation symbols can be viewed as propositional variables, we can think of the syntax of first-order logic as including that of propositional logic. It is often clearer to write binary relations like \( < \) using infix notation, but that should be viewed as a convention taking place in the metatheory.

Define the set of free variables of a formula recursively, as follows:

- variables \( \left(  \bot  \right)  = \varnothing \) .

- variables \( \left( {s = t}\right)  = \) variables \( \left( s\right)  \cup \) variables \( \left( t\right) \) .

- variables \( \left( {R\left( {{t}_{0},\ldots ,{t}_{k - 1}}\right) }\right)  = \operatorname{variables}\left( {t}_{0}\right)  \cup  \ldots  \cup  \operatorname{variables}\left( {t}_{k - 1}\right) \) .

- variables \( \left( {A \circ  B}\right)  = \operatorname{variables}\left( A\right)  \cup  \operatorname{variables}\left( B\right) \) , where \( \circ \) is any of the propositional connectives.

- variables \( \left( {\forall {xA}}\right)  = \operatorname{variables}\left( {\exists {xA}}\right)  = \operatorname{variables}\left( A\right)  \smallsetminus  \{ x\} \) .

The last clause reflects the fact that \( x \) becomes bound in \( \forall {xA} \) and \( \exists {xA} \) , and hence is no longer a free variable. Indeed, the definition would be ill-posed if we did not eliminate \( x \) , since it would depend on the choice of a representative of \( \forall {xA} \) among expressions that are considered equivalent up to renaming of their bound variables.

There is a useful convention that allows us to treat formulas with free variables as though they are functions that return propositions. Saying "let \( A\left( x\right) \) be a formula" specifies that \( A \) is a formula and, at the same time, highlights a particular variable \( x \) that may or may not occur in \( A \) . Later references to \( A\left( t\right) \) are then interpreted as \( A\left\lbrack  {t/x}\right\rbrack \) . A similar understanding applies to terms, which is to say, if \( t \) has been introduced as \( t\left( x\right) \) , the expression \( t\left( s\right) \) subsequently denotes \( t\left\lbrack  {s/x}\right\rbrack \) . These conventions should bring to mind the standard mathematical practice of defining a function \( f\left( x\right) \) with an expression like \( {x}^{2} + {bx} + c \) , after which \( f\left( u\right) \) denotes the result of substituting \( u \) for \( x \) in the expression. Multiple substitutions in formulas \( A\left( {{x}_{0},\ldots ,{x}_{k - 1}}\right) \) and terms \( t\left( {{x}_{0},\ldots ,{x}_{k - 1}}\right) \) are dealt with in a similar way, using the notion of simultaneous substitution, as described in Section 1.5. I will often write \( \overrightarrow{x} \) to denote a tuple of variables \( {x}_{0},\ldots ,{x}_{k - 1} \) and \( \overrightarrow{t} \) to denote a tuple of terms \( {t}_{0},\ldots ,{t}_{k - 1} \) .

### 4.2 Quantifiers

When it comes to first-order logic, most of the deductive apparatus from propositional logic carries over with little change. But how shall we interpret a proof of a formula \( A \) when \( A \) has free variable? We will think of a free variable \( x \) of \( A \) as representing an arbitrary, unspecified object, and therefore understand the proof as showing that \( A \) is true for any value of \( x \) . On that view, we are allowed to conclude \( \forall {xA} \) from a proof of \( A \) .

To obtain axiomatic systems for minimal, intuitionistic, and classical logic, we now read the axioms of Section 2.2 as schemas involving first-order formulas. We add the following axioms:

- \( \forall {xA} \rightarrow  A\left\lbrack  {t/x}\right\rbrack \)

- \( A\left\lbrack  {t/x}\right\rbrack   \rightarrow  \exists {xA} \) .

In addition to modus ponens, we now have the following rules:

- From \( A \rightarrow  B \) conclude \( A \rightarrow  \forall {xB} \) , provided \( x \) is not free in \( A \) .

- From \( A \rightarrow  B \) conclude \( \exists {xA} \rightarrow  B \) , provided \( x \) is not free in \( B \) .

The first of these two rules is a slight generalization of the rule "from \( B \) , conclude \( \forall {xB} \) ." The difference is that we are allowed to do this modulo a hypothesis, \( A \) , that does not depend on \( x \) .

The rule for the existential quantifier is dual; if \( B \) follows from the assumption that \( A \) holds of an arbitrary \( x \) , then \( B \) follows from \( \exists {xB} \) . In these rules, the side conditions are sometimes called eigenvariable conditions.

Presentations that do not identify formulas up to renaming of bound variables typically allow variables to be renamed when applying the quantifier rules. In such a presentation, the rules read as follows:

- From \( A \rightarrow  B \) conclude \( A \rightarrow  \forall {yB}\left\lbrack  {y/x}\right\rbrack \) , provided \( x \) is not free in \( A \) , and \( y \) is not free in \( B \) unless \( y = x \) .

- From \( A \rightarrow  B \) conclude \( \exists {yA}\left\lbrack  {y/x}\right\rbrack   \rightarrow  B \) , provided \( x \) is not free in \( B \) , and \( y \) is not free in \( A \) unless \( y = x \) .

The restrictions ensure that the new bound variable \( y \) is fresh. With our conventions, however, \( \forall {yB}\left\lbrack  {y/x}\right\rbrack \) and \( \forall {xB} \) are exactly the same formula, so we can adopt the simpler formulations.

We have to be careful in defining the notion of provability from hypotheses: what are we to make of a hypothesis, \( A \) , that has a free variable, \( x \) ? One option is to view \( x \) as, essentially, a constant whose value is fixed throughout the proof. On this view, the conclusion \( \forall {xA} \) is not warranted. Another option is to view \( A \) as asserting its universal closure, i.e. the formula \( \forall \overrightarrow{z}A \) where \( \overrightarrow{z} \) is a list of all the free variables of \( A \) in some fixed order. In that case \( \forall {xA} \) does follow. For the time being we will avoid ambiguity and confusion by restricting the notion of provability to hypotheses that are sentences. Then, as before, we will say \( \Gamma  \vdash  A \) if and only if there is a proof of \( A \) with hypotheses in \( \Gamma \) , where a proof is a sequence of formulas in which each formula either is a hypothesis, is an axiom, or follows from previous formulas by one of the rules of inference.

To extend our systems of natural deduction to first-order logic, add the following rules:

\[
\frac{A}{\forall {xA}}\forall \mathrm{I}\;\frac{\forall {xA}}{A\left\lbrack  {t/x}\right\rbrack  }\forall \mathrm{E}
\]

In the introduction rule, we place the restriction that \( x \) is not free in any open hypothesis, which corresponds to the eigenvalue condition above. Similarly, we have the rules for the existential quantifier:

Once again, in the elimination rule, we assume that \( x \) is not free in \( B \) or any hypothesis other than \( A \) . The elimination rule exhibits a pattern of reasoning that is similar to that of disjunction elimination: to show that \( B \) holds on assumption \( \exists {xA} \) , let \( x \) be an arbitrary object satisfying \( A \) and show that \( B \) follows.

In sequent form, the natural deduction rules are expressed as follows.

\[
\frac{\Gamma  \vdash  A}{\Gamma  \vdash  \forall {xA}}\forall \mathrm{R}\;\frac{\Gamma  \vdash  \forall {xA}}{\Gamma  \vdash  A\left\lbrack  {t/x}\right\rbrack  }\forall \mathrm{E}
\]

\[
\frac{\Gamma  \vdash  A\left\lbrack  {t/x}\right\rbrack  }{\Gamma  \vdash  \exists {xA}}\exists \mathrm{I}\;\frac{\Gamma  \vdash  \exists {xA}\;\Gamma , A \vdash  B}{\Gamma  \vdash  B}\exists \mathrm{E}
\]

The set of sequents provable in natural deduction is defined, as before, as the set that is generated inductively by these rules. If \( \Gamma \) is a set of sentences and \( A \) is any formula, we can show as usual that \( \Gamma \) proves \( A \) if and only if there is a natural deduction proof of \( {\Gamma }^{\prime } \vdash  A \) for some finite \( {\Gamma }^{\prime } \subseteq  \Gamma \) .

If \( \Gamma  \vdash  A\left( x\right) \) , then \( \Gamma  \vdash  \forall {xA}\left( x\right) \) , and hence \( \Gamma  \vdash  A\left( c\right) \) for any constant symbol \( c \) . Conversely, if \( c \) does not occur in \( \Gamma \) or \( A\left( x\right) \) and \( \Gamma  \vdash  A\left( c\right) \) , we can think of \( c \) as being arbitrary. As a result, in first-order logic, there isn't much of a difference between a variable and a fresh constant. The following proposition makes this precise.

Proposition 4.2.1. Let \( \Gamma \) be a set of sentences, let \( A\left( x\right) \) be a formula, and let \( c \) be a constant that does not occur in \( \Gamma \) or \( A \) . If \( \Gamma  \vdash  A\left( c\right) \) , then \( \Gamma  \vdash  A\left( x\right) \) .

The idea is that given a proof of \( A\left( c\right) \) from some finite subset of \( \Gamma \) , we can replace \( c \) by \( x \) everywhere in the proof. This is not quite correct: if \( x \) occurs somewhere in the original proof and is eliminated by a quantifier rule, then replacing \( c \) by \( x \) might violate an eigenvariable condition. A solution is to first replace \( c \) by a variable \( y \) which does not occur anywhere in the proof, thereby avoiding such conflicts. This results in a proof of \( A\left( y\right) \) from \( \Gamma \) . From that, we can derive \( \forall {yA}\left( y\right) \) and \( A\left( x\right) \) .

Carrying out the proof rigorously requires defining what it means for a variable to occur in a proof. The following lemma provides another approach which renames eigenvariables as needed. It is tailored specifically to natural deduction, but it has Proposition 4.2.1 as an immediate corollary. In the statement of the lemma, if \( \overrightarrow{x} \) is any tuple of variables, \( \overrightarrow{c} \) is any tuple of constants, and \( \overrightarrow{t} \) and \( \overrightarrow{s} \) are tuples of terms the same length as \( \overrightarrow{x} \) and \( \overrightarrow{c} \) respectively, we write \( A\left\lbrack  {\overrightarrow{t}/\overrightarrow{x},\overrightarrow{s}/\overrightarrow{c}}\right\rbrack \) for the result of simultaneously substituting those terms for the corresponding variables and constants. Similarly, we write \( \Gamma \left\lbrack  {\overrightarrow{t}/\overrightarrow{x},\overrightarrow{s}/\overrightarrow{c}}\right\rbrack \) for \( \{ B\left\lbrack  {\overrightarrow{t}/\overrightarrow{x},\overrightarrow{s}/\overrightarrow{c}}\right\rbrack   \mid  B \in  \Gamma \} \) .

Then for any \( \overrightarrow{x},\overrightarrow{c},\overrightarrow{s} \) , and \( \overrightarrow{t} \) , \( \Gamma \left\lbrack  {\overrightarrow{t}/\overrightarrow{x},\overrightarrow{s}/\overrightarrow{c}}\right\rbrack   \vdash  A\left\lbrack  {\overrightarrow{t}/\overrightarrow{x},\overrightarrow{s}/\overrightarrow{c}}\right\rbrack  . \)

The only interesting cases are \( \forall \) introduction and \( \exists \) elimination. For the first of these, suppose the last rule obtains \( \Gamma  \vdash  \forall {yB} \) from \( \Gamma  \vdash  B \) . By the eigenvariable condition, \( y \) does not occur in \( \Gamma \) . Let \( z \) be a variable that does not occur in \( \Gamma , B,\overrightarrow{x},\overrightarrow{t} \) , or \( \overrightarrow{s} \) . By the inductive hypothesis, there is a derivation of \( \Gamma \left\lbrack  {\overrightarrow{t}/\overrightarrow{x}, z/y,\overrightarrow{s}/\overrightarrow{c}}\right\rbrack   \vdash  B\left\lbrack  {\overrightarrow{t}/\overrightarrow{x}, z/y,\overrightarrow{s}/\overrightarrow{c}}\right\rbrack \) , and since \( y \) does not occur in \( \Gamma \) , the antecedent is \( \Gamma \left\lbrack  {\overrightarrow{t}/\overrightarrow{x},\overrightarrow{s}/\overrightarrow{c}}\right\rbrack \) . \]

\[

The next two propositions present key properties of the first-order quantifiers. In two instances, where only one direction of a classical equivalence holds in minimal logic, we write \( A \leftarrow  B \) for \( B \rightarrow  A \) .

Proposition 4.2.3. The following are derivable in minimal logic:

1. \( \forall {xA} \leftrightarrow  A \) if \( x \) is not free in \( A \)

2. \( \exists {xA} \leftrightarrow  A \) if \( x \) is not free in \( A \)

3. \( \forall x\left( {A \land  B}\right)  \leftrightarrow  \forall {xA} \land  \forall {xB} \)

4. \( \exists x\left( {A \land  B}\right)  \leftrightarrow  \exists {xA} \land  B \) if \( x \) is not free in \( B \)

5. \( \forall x\left( {A \vee  B}\right)  \leftarrow  \forall {xA} \vee  B \) if \( x \) is not free in \( B \)

6. \( \exists x\left( {A \vee  B}\right)  \leftrightarrow  \exists {xA} \vee  \exists {xB} \)

7. \( \forall x\left( {A \rightarrow  B}\right)  \leftrightarrow  \left( {\exists {xA} \rightarrow  B}\right) \) if \( x \) is not free in \( B \)

8. \( \exists x\left( {A \rightarrow  B}\right)  \rightarrow  \left( {\forall {xA} \rightarrow  B}\right) \) if \( x \) is not free in \( B \)

9. \( \forall x\left( {A \rightarrow  B}\right)  \leftrightarrow  \left( {A \rightarrow  \forall {xB}}\right) \) if \( x \) is not free in \( A \)

10. \( \exists x\left( {A \rightarrow  B}\right)  \rightarrow  \left( {A \rightarrow  \exists {xB}}\right) \) if \( x \) is not free in \( A \)

11. \( \neg \exists {xA} \leftrightarrow  \forall x\neg A \)

12. \( \neg \forall {xA} \leftarrow  \exists x\neg A \) .

Proposition 4.2.4 shows that including both \( \forall \) and \( \exists \) among the basic connectives of classical logic is redundant. If we express all our formulas in terms of \( \forall \) , the following provides what is perhaps the quickest deductive characterization of classical provability in first-order logic: as axioms, take

1. any substitution instance of a propositional tautology, and

2. \( \forall {xA} \rightarrow  A\left\lbrack  {t/x}\right\rbrack \)

and for rules, allow

1. modus ponens: from \( A \rightarrow  B \) and \( A \) conclude \( B \)

2. generalization: from \( A \rightarrow  B \) conclude \( A \rightarrow  \forall {xB} \) , as long as \( x \) is not free in \( A \) .

In the exercises below you are asked to show that if we modify the axioms, we can get by with only one rule, modus ponens.

That our axioms and rules presuppose that the domain of discourse is nonempty can be seen from the fact that \( \forall {xA}\left( x\right)  \rightarrow  \exists {xA}\left( x\right) \) is derivable for any formula \( A \) , as well as \( \exists x\left( {A\left( x\right)  \rightarrow  A\left( x\right) }\right) \) . (When we have the rules for equality, we will see that \( \exists x\left( {x = x}\right) \) is also derivable.) In Section 4.8, we will consider variants of first-order logic that allow for the possibility of an empty domain.

First-order logic is often used to model the notion of logical consequence in the context of scientific reasoning. From a logical perspective, a first-order theory is any set of sentences that is closed under the provability relation. In other words, a theory is a set \( T \) such that whenever \( T \) proves \( A, A \) is already in \( T \) . As a consequence of the transitivity of the provability relation, it is easy to check that if \( \Gamma \) is any set of sentences, then the set \( T = \{ A \mid  \Gamma  \vdash  A\} \) is a theory. This set \( T \) is called the theory axiomatized by \( \Gamma \) .

It is often useful to classify first-order formulas in terms of the structure of the patterns of quantifiers they use. A first-order formula is said to be universal if it consists of a block of universal quantifiers (possibly empty) followed by a quantifier-free formula. Replacing "universal" by "existential" yields the notion of an existential formula. A theory that is axiomatized by a set of universal formulas is said to be universally axiomatized. We will have more to say about such theories in Section 7.1.

### 4.3 Equality

In first-order logic, the expression \( s = t \) is used to say that \( s \) and \( t \) denote the same object, or, at least, entities that are indistinguishable given the linguistic resources at hand. In other words, we assume that equality is an equivalence relation and that it is a congruence with respect to all the function and relation symbols in the language. These properties are expressed by the universal closures of the following axioms:

1. \( x = x \)

2. \( x = y \rightarrow  y = x \)

3. \( x = y \land  y = z \rightarrow  x = z \)

4. \( \overrightarrow{x} = \overrightarrow{y} \rightarrow  f\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right)  = f\left( \overrightarrow{y}\right) \) for each function symbol \( f \)

5. \( \overrightarrow{x} = \overrightarrow{y} \rightarrow  \left( {R\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right)  \rightarrow  R\left( \overrightarrow{y}\right) }\right) \) for each relation symbol \( R \) .

Here \( \overrightarrow{x} \) and \( \overrightarrow{y} \) denote tuples of variables of the appropriate lengths, and I have written \( \overrightarrow{x} = \overrightarrow{y} \) for \( k \) -tuples \( \overrightarrow{x} \) and \( \overrightarrow{y} \) instead of \( {x}_{0} = {y}_{0} \land  \cdots  \land  {x}_{k - 1} = {y}_{k - 1} \) . We can assume \( R \) is not the equality symbol in 5; conversely, Exercise 4.3.2 shows that if we allow \( R \) to be equality then we can dispense with axioms 2 and 3. Exercise 4.3.3 shows that it suffices instead to use the following axiom and axiom schema:

1. \( x = x \)

2. \( x = y \rightarrow  \left( {A\left( x\right)  \rightarrow  A\left( y\right) }\right) \) for any atomic formula \( A \) .

The important observation is that we do not need to state equality axioms explicitly for arbitrary terms and formulas:

Proposition 4.3.1. The following are provable (in minimal, intuitionistic, and classical logic) for any term \( t\left( x\right) \) and formula \( A\left( x\right) \) :

1. \( x = y \rightarrow  t\left( x\right)  = t\left( y\right) \)

2. \( x = y \rightarrow  \left( {A\left( x\right)  \rightarrow  A\left( y\right) }\right) \) .

Proof The first claim is proved by induction on terms and the second is proved by induction on formulas.

In systems of natural deduction, it is more natural to express the axioms above as rules, allowing arbitrary instances of terms in place of the variables:

\[
\frac{}{t = t}\;\frac{s = t}{t = s}\;\frac{s = t\;t = u}{s = u}
\]

\[
\frac{\overrightarrow{s} = \overrightarrow{t}}{f\left( \overrightarrow{s}\right)  = f\left( \overrightarrow{t}\right) }\;\frac{\overrightarrow{s} = \overrightarrow{t}\;R\left( \overrightarrow{s}\right) }{R\left( \overrightarrow{t}\right) }
\]

Here \( \overrightarrow{s} \) and \( \overrightarrow{t} \) denote arbitrary tuples of terms of appropriate length, and the bar over the reflexivity rule indicates that the rule has no premises. As above, we can use the following rules instead, where \( A\left( x\right) \) is any atomic formula:

\[
\frac{}{t = t}\;\frac{s = t\;A\left( s\right) }{A\left( t\right) }
\]

Proposition 4.3.1 tells us that the following are derived rules for any term \( r\left( x\right) \) and any formula \( A\left( x\right) \) :

\[
\frac{s = t}{r\left( s\right)  = r\left( t\right) }\frac{s = t\;A\left( s\right) }{A\left( t\right) }
\]

The initial five rules provide a useful characterization of equality, but when it comes to using a deductive system for equality, it is nice to have the last two substitution rules, and, perhaps, symmetric versions with \( s = t \) replaced by \( t = s \) .

We can study the rules of equality in isolation. Consider a system of natural deduction in which every sequent is of the form \( {u}_{0} = {v}_{0},\ldots ,{u}_{n - 1} = {v}_{n - 1} \vdash  s = t \) . We allow only the first four of the basic equality rules listed above, that is, reflexivity, symmetry, transitivity, and the congruence rules for the function symbols. We also include the assumption rule, as usual. I will refer to this as the equational fragment of first-order logic. In a sequent formulation, we can require the same hypotheses in both premises of a binary rule, so, for example, transitivity is written as follows:

\[
\frac{\Gamma  \vdash  r = s\;\Gamma  \vdash  s = t}{\Gamma  \vdash  r = t}
\]

Alternatively, we can keep track of the hypotheses that are actually used, using \( s = t \vdash  s = t \) for the assumption rule and writing transitivity as follows:

\[
\frac{\Gamma  \vdash  r = s\;\Delta  \vdash  s = t}{\Gamma ,\Delta  \vdash  r = t}
\]

For consistency with our presentation of natural deduction, we will follow the first approach.

We will see in Chapter 5 that this proof system is complete, in the sense that if \( \Gamma  \cup  \{ A\} \) consists of equations between closed terms and \( A \) holds in every structure that satisfies \( \Gamma \) , the \( A \) is derivable in the system above. This implies that if a sequent \( \Gamma  \vdash  A \) is provable with all of first-order logic, using either the classical, intuitionistic, or minimal version, it is derivable from the equality rules alone. In Chapter 6, we will have a purely syntactic proof of this fact.

Even without this strong result, however, we can derive an important property of the system.

Theorem 4.3.2. If a sequent \( \Gamma  \vdash  A \) is provable in the equational fragment of first-order logic, there is a proof in which every term that appears occurs as a subterm of some formula in \( \Gamma  \cup  \{ A\} \) .

Proof First, notice that in any proof of an equation \( s = t \) , we can permute the symmetry rule upward so that it is only applied to hypotheses. For example, the pattern on the left, consisting of a transitivity inference followed by symmetry, can be replaced by the pattern on the right:

\[
\frac{s = r\;r = t}{\frac{s = t}{t = s}}\;\frac{r = t\;\frac{s = r}{t = r}}{t = s}\;\frac{s = r}{t = s}
\]

A similar transformation works for instances of the congruence rule followed by symmetry, and iterating these transformations has the desired effect. Henceforth, we therefore restrict attention to proofs in which symmetry is only applied at hypotheses.

The main difficulty in proving Theorem 4.3.2 involves the instances of transitivity:

\[
\frac{\Gamma  \vdash  s = r\;\Gamma  \vdash  r = t}{\Gamma  \vdash  s = t}
\]

The problem is that the middle term, \( r \) , vanishes from the proof, and if \( r \) is not a subterm of a formula in \( \Gamma \) , there is nothing that guarantees that it will be a subterm of the conclusion. Call a proof good if, for every instance of such a transitivity rule, \( r \) is indeed a subterm of a formula in \( \Gamma \) . It is not hard to show, by induction, that a good proof of a sequent \( \Gamma  \vdash  A \) has the property stated in the theorem.

To prove the theorem, we first prove the following auxiliary claim: given good proofs of \( \Gamma  \vdash  s = r \) and \( \Gamma  \vdash  r = t \) , there is a good proof of \( \Gamma  \vdash  s = t \) . To do so, we use induction on the sum of the number of nodes in the two proofs.

If the proof of either \( \Gamma  \vdash  s = r \) or \( \Gamma  \vdash  r = t \) is an instance of reflexivity, then \( r \) is identical with either \( s \) or \( t \) and the other proof is the one we want. Similarly, if either is a hypothesis or symmetry applied to a hypothesis, then \( r \) is a subterm of a formula in \( \Gamma \) , and we can apply transitivity to obtain a good proof of \( \Gamma  \vdash  s = t \) .

Suppose one of the subproofs - say, the left one - is again an instance of transitivity, so that applying transitivity would result in a proof that ends like so:

\[
\frac{s = u\;u = r}{s = r\;r = t}
\]

We can permute the transitivity rules like so:

\[
\begin{matrix} s = u\;\frac{u = r\;r = t}{u = t} \\  s = t \end{matrix}
\]

By the hypothesis that the original proof of \( s = r \) is good, \( u \) is a subterm of a formula in \( \Gamma \) . Since the proof of \( u = r \) is smaller than the proof of \( s = r \) , we can apply the inductive hypothesis to the pair on the right and obtain a good proof of \( u = t \) . By hypothesis, the proof of \( s = u \) is also good, so we can apply transitivity to obtain a good proof.

The only case remaining is where both the final inferences are congruences, so that applying transitivity would result in a proof that ends like so:

\[
\begin{matrix} {s}_{0} = {r}_{0} & \cdots & {s}_{n - 1} = {r}_{n - 1} \\   & f\left( \overrightarrow{s}\right)  = f\left( \overrightarrow{r}\right) & f\left( \overrightarrow{s}\right)  = f\left( \overrightarrow{t}\right) \\   & f\left( \overrightarrow{s}\right)  = f\left( \overrightarrow{t}\right) & f\left( \overrightarrow{r}\right)  = f\left( \overrightarrow{t}\right)  \end{matrix}
\]

In this case, we can again permute the transitivity rule upward:

\[
\begin{matrix} {s}_{0} = {r}_{0} & {r}_{0} = {t}_{0} & {s}_{n - 1} = {r}_{n - 1} & {r}_{n - 1} = {t}_{n - 1} \\   & {s}_{0} = {t}_{0} & \ldots & {s}_{n - 1} = {t}_{n - 1} \\   & f\left( \overrightarrow{s}\right)  = f\left( \overrightarrow{t}\right) & &  \end{matrix}
\]

The fact that every provable sequent \( \Gamma  \vdash  A \) has a good proof now follows by an easy induction on the derivation of \( \Gamma  \vdash  A \) . Reflexivity, a hypothesis, or a hypothesis followed by symmetry are all good proofs, and applying a congruence rule preserves goodness. The only other case is the transitivity rule, which is handled by the claim that we just proved.

This provides us with an algorithm to determine whether or not the sequent \( \Gamma  \vdash  A \) is provable in the equational fragment of first-order logic: make a list of all the subterms occurring in \( \Gamma \) and \( A \) , start with the hypotheses in \( \Gamma \) , and then iteratively apply rules of inference to derive new equalities between subterms, until A has been proved or there are no more equalities to be had. In the latter case, Theorem 4.3.2 guarantees that the sequent is not provable.

### 4.4 Equational and Quantifier-Free Logic

It is sometimes useful to consider an extension of the equational fragment of first-order logic where the hypotheses are taken to be implicitly universally quantified. For example, a group is a structure for a language with a constant symbol, 1, a binary operation, -, and a unary inverse, satisfying the following axioms:

---

	- \( \forall x, y, z\left( {\left( {x \cdot  y}\right)  \cdot  z = x \cdot  \left( {y \cdot  z}\right) }\right) \)

- \( \forall x\left( {x \cdot  1 = x}\right) \)

- \( \forall x\left( {1 \cdot  x = x}\right) \)

	- \( \forall x\left( {x \cdot  {x}^{-1} = 1}\right) \)

	- \( \forall x\left( {{x}^{-1} \cdot  x = 1}\right) \) .

---

We may want to have a proof system for deriving equations from these axioms. We can leave off the quantifiers but still have the effect of treating formulas with variables as implicitly universally quantified if we add a substitution rule,

\[
\frac{A\left( x\right) }{A\left( t\right) }
\]

for any term \( t \) . The result of adding the substitution rule to the equational fragment of first-order logic is known as equational logic. We have the following:

Theorem 4.4.1. Let \( \Gamma \) be a set of equations and let \( s = t \) be an equation. Then the following are equivalent:

1. \( s = t \) is provable from substitution instances of \( \Gamma \) in the equational fragment of first-order logic.

2. \( s = t \) is provable from \( \Gamma \) in equational logic.

3. \( s = t \) is provable from the universal closures of formulas in \( \Gamma \) in classical first-order logic.

The implication from 1 to 2 is immediate, since we can use the substitution rule to obtain substitution instances of any formula in \( \Gamma \) . The implication from 2 to 3 is also immediate, since we can use the \( \forall \) elimination rule to instantiate universal quantifiers. It is not hard to prove 1 from 2 by induction on derivations. The implication from 3 to 1 and 2 is the most interesting, since it shows that even adding full classical logic with quantifiers does not change the provable entailments between equations. We will see a model-theoretic proof of this in Section 5.2 and a proof-theoretic proof in Section 6.6.

Although the terminological distinction between equational logic and the equational fragment of first-order logic is subtle, the difference is substantial. We saw in the last section that the question as to whether something is provable in the equational fragment of first-order logic is algorithmically decidable. This is no longer true when we add the substitution rule. Roughly speaking, the problem is that we cannot predict in advance which substitution instances of the hypotheses might be needed to prove the conclusion. In computer science and universal algebra, the problem of determining whether two first-order terms are equal modulo a set of identities is known as the word problem. For example, the word problem for groups is as follows: given a finite set \( S \) of identities between closed terms in the language of groups and an equation \( s = t \) , does \( s = t \) hold in the free group modulo \( S \) , and hence in all groups satisfying \( S \) ? It is known that the word problem for groups is undecidable, which is to say, there is no algorithm that can reliably answer that question in general. For more information, see the discussion at the end of Section 12.5 and the notes to Chapter 12.

Just as it is sometimes useful to isolate the equational fragment of first-order logic, it is also sometimes useful to isolate the quantifier-free fragment of first-order logic, that is, the rules for the propositional connectives and equality. In analogy with equational logic, I will use the phrase quantifier-free logic to refer to the quantifier-free fragment of first-order logic together with the substitution rule. In analogy to Theorem 4.4.1, we have the following relationship between quantifier-free logic and the quantifier-free fragment of first-order logic:

Theorem 4.4.2. Let \( \Gamma \) be a set of quantifier-free formulas and let \( A \) be a quantifier-free formula. Then the following are equivalent:

1. A is provable from substitution instances of \( \Gamma \) in the quantifier-free fragment of first-order logic.

2. A is provable from \( \Gamma \) in quantifier-free logic.

3. A is provable from the universal closures of formulas in \( \Gamma \) in full first-order logic.

In contrast to Theorem 4.4.1, here we need to specify whether we are talking about classical, intuitionistic, or minimal logic, since "quantifier-free logic" means something different in each case. For classical logic, Section 5.2 provides a model-theoretic proof, and Section 7.1 provides a proof-theoretic proof. The latter can be straightforwardly adapted to intuitionistic and minimal logic as well.

Recall from Section 4.2 that a formula is universal if it consists of a block of universal quantifiers followed by a quantifier-free formula. When we prove a formula \( A \) with free variables, the intended interpretation is that the conclusion holds for any value that can be assigned to the variables. So we can think of quantifier-free logic as a means of deriving (implicitly) universal conclusions from (implicitly) universal hypotheses.

We observed above that the word problem for groups is undecidable. Since the theory of groups can be axiomatized by a finite set of identities, we have the undecidability of this more general version of the word problem:

Theorem 4.4.3. The question as to whether an equation follows from a finite set of identities in first-order logic is undecidable.

Since equality itself can be axiomatized by a finite set of universal formulas, this implies that the question as to whether a quantifier-free formula can be derived from a finite set of universal axioms in first-order logic without equality is also undecidable.

But the question as to whether a quantifier-free formula, \( A \) , is derivable in pure first-order logic, without any additional axioms, is decidable. By Theorem 4.4.2, the question is equivalent to asking whether \( A \) is derivable in the quantifier-free fragment of first-order logic. In fact, Section 6.6 shows that it suffices to consider instances of the equality axioms for subterms of terms occurring in \( A \) , of which there are only finitely many. (Exercise 5.2.2 suggests a model-theoretic argument for the classical case.) This reduces the problem to the decidability of propositional logic. Thus we have:

Theorem 4.4.4. The universal fragment of first-order logic with equality is decidable.

In other words, there is an algorithm for determining whether a universal formula is valid. In the field of automated reasoning, practical implementations are usually based on an algorithm known as congruence closure.

### 4.5 Normal Forms for Classical Logic

As with propositional logic, when dealing with classical first-order logic it is often useful to express formulas in various normal forms. For example, we can always eliminate \( \exists \) in favor of \( \forall \) or vice versa, and we can restrict the propositional connectives to any complete set. The definition of negation normal form carries over from Section 2.6; in the first-order setting the set of formulas in negation normal form is defined to be the smallest set of formulas that contains all the atomic formulas and their negations and is closed under conjunction, disjunction, and application of the universal and existential quantifiers. We extend the definition of the negation operator with the clauses \( \sim  \left( {\forall {xA}}\right)  \equiv  \exists x\left( { \sim  A}\right) \) and \( \sim  \left( {\exists {xA}}\right)  \equiv  \forall x\left( { \sim  A}\right) \) .

A formula is said to be in prenex form if it consists of a string of quantifiers followed by a quantifier-free formula. More formally, the set of prenex formulas is defined to be the smallest set of formulas containing all the quantifier-free formulas and closed under universal and existential quantification. Classically, we can always put a formula in prenex form by putting it in negation normal form and then using these rules, which hold when \( x \) is not free in \( B \) :

\[
\forall {xA} \vee  B \equiv  \forall x\left( {A \vee  B}\right)
\]

\[
\forall {xA} \land  B \equiv  \forall x\left( {A \land  B}\right)
\]

\[
\exists {xA} \vee  B \equiv  \exists x\left( {A \vee  B}\right)
\]

\[
\exists {xA} \land  B \equiv  \exists x\left( {A \land  B}\right) ,
\]

We can always rename bound variables to ensure that these rules apply. Of course, it is not necessary to put a formula in negation normal form in order to bring the quantifiers to the front; for example, we can use the equivalences

\[
\forall {xA} \rightarrow  B \equiv  \exists x\left( {A \rightarrow  B}\right)
\]

\[
\exists {xA} \rightarrow  B \equiv  \forall x\left( {A \rightarrow  B}\right)
\]

\[
A \rightarrow  \forall {xB} \equiv  \forall x\left( {A \rightarrow  B}\right)
\]

\[
A \rightarrow  \exists {xB} \equiv  \exists x\left( {A \rightarrow  B}\right)
\]

to bring quantifiers to the front of an implication.

Though it is often convenient, a formula's prenex form is only a crude representation of its logical structure. In general, the prenex form is not unique, since one has to make choices as to which quantifiers to bring to the front first. One typically minimizes the number of alternations between blocks of universal and existential quantifiers, but even that restriction leaves room for variation. For example, the formula \( \exists {xA}\left( x\right)  \rightarrow  \exists {yB}\left( y\right) \) is equivalent to both \( \forall x\exists y\left( {A\left( x\right)  \rightarrow  B\left( y\right) }\right) \) and \( \exists y\forall x\left( {A\left( x\right)  \rightarrow  B\left( y\right) }\right) \) .

### 4.6 Translations Between Logics

In Section 2.7, we considered translations between the classical, intuitionistic, and minimal versions of propositional logic. We now extend these to first-order logic. The interpretation of intuitionistic logic in minimal logic goes through with few changes. If we let \( {A}^{ * } \) denote the result of replacing each atomic subformula \( C \) of \( A \) (other than \( \bot \) ) by \( C \vee   \bot \) , we still have:

Theorem 4.6.1. Let \( \Gamma \) be any set of sentences and let \( A \) be any formula.

1. \( { \vdash  }_{\mathrm{I}}A \leftrightarrow  {A}^{ * } \) .

2. If \( \Gamma { \vdash  }_{\mathrm{I}}A \) , then \( {\Gamma }^{ * }{ \vdash  }_{\mathrm{M}}{A}^{ * } \) .

We can extend the Gödel-Gentzen double-negation translation to first-order logic with the following clauses:

- \( {\left( \forall xA\right) }^{\mathrm{N}} = \forall x{A}^{\mathrm{N}} \)

- \( {\left( \exists xA\right) }^{\mathrm{N}} = \neg \forall x\neg {A}^{\mathrm{N}} \) .

In minimal logic, \( \neg \forall x\neg A \) is equivalent to \( \neg \neg \exists {xA} \) . But with the choice of \( {\left( \exists xA\right) }^{\mathrm{N}} \) above, \( {A}^{\mathrm{N}} \) is always negative, in the following sense.

Definition 4.6.2. A formula is negative if \( \vee \) and \( \exists \) do not occur in it and every atomic formula other than \( \bot \) is negated.

Equivalently, the set of negative formulas is the smallest set containing \( \bot \) and negated atomic formulas, and closed under \( \land  , \rightarrow \) , and \( \forall \) . As in Section 2.7, we can show that, for any negative formula \( A \) , the formula \( \neg \neg A \rightarrow  A \) is provable in minimal logic. The only new case is handled using the fact that \( \neg \neg \forall {xA} \rightarrow  \forall x\neg \neg A \) is provable there. We will see in Section 5.3 that the converse direction, \( \forall x\neg \neg A \rightarrow  \neg \neg \forall {xA} \) , is not generally derivable in minimal or even intuitionistic logic. This implication is known as the double-negation shift. As in Section 2.7, we have the following theorem and corollary.

Theorem 4.6.3. Let \( \Gamma \) be any set of sentences and let \( A \) be any formula.

1. \( { \vdash  }_{\mathrm{C}}A \leftrightarrow  {A}^{\mathrm{N}} \) .

2. If \( \Gamma { \vdash  }_{\mathrm{C}}A \) then \( {\Gamma }^{\mathrm{N}}{ \vdash  }_{\mathrm{M}}{A}^{\mathrm{N}} \) .

Corollary 4.6.4. Classical logic is conservative over minimal logic for negative formulas: if \( { \vdash  }_{\mathrm{C}}A \) and \( A \) is negative, then \( { \vdash  }_{\mathrm{M}}A \) .

There are many variations on the double-negation translation. Glivenko's theorem does not hold for first-order logic, but we can still reduce the number of negations that are added. Define a translation inductively as follows:

\[
{A}_{\mathrm{K}} = A
\]

\[
{\left( A \circ  B\right) }_{\mathrm{K}} = {A}_{\mathrm{K}} \circ  {B}_{\mathrm{K}}
\]

\[
{\left( \exists xA\right) }_{\mathrm{K}} = \exists x{A}_{\mathrm{K}}
\]

\[
{\left( \forall xA\right) }_{\mathrm{K}} = \forall x\neg \neg {A}_{\mathrm{K}}.
\]

In the first clause, \( A \) is assumed to be atomic, and in the second clause, \( \circ \) stands for any of the binary propositional connectives. Define the Kuroda translation of \( A \) , denoted \( {A}^{\mathrm{K}} \) , to be \( \neg \neg {A}_{\mathrm{K}} \) . So the Kuroda translation inserts a double-negation at the beginning of the formula and after each universal quantifier.

Lemma 4.6.5. For every formula \( A,{ \vdash  }_{\mathrm{I}}{A}^{\mathrm{K}} \leftrightarrow  {A}^{\mathrm{N}} \) .

The proof proceeds by induction on \( A \) . If \( \Gamma \) is a set sentences, as usual, let \( {\Gamma }^{\mathrm{K}} \) denote \( \left\{  {{A}^{\mathrm{K}} \mid  }\right. \; A \in  \Gamma \} \) . Together with Theorem 4.6.3, this yields:

Theorem 4.6.6 (Kuroda’s theorem). For every formula \( A \) and set of sentences \( \Gamma \) , if \( \Gamma { \vdash  }_{\mathrm{C}}A \) then \( {\Gamma }^{\mathrm{K}}{ \vdash  }_{\mathrm{I}}{A}^{\mathrm{K}} \) .

Note that, as with Glivenko's theorem, intuitionistic logic rather than minimal is required in the conclusion of Kuroda's theorem.

The following translation works on formulas expressed in terms of the connectives \( \{ \neg , \land  , \vee  ,\forall ,\exists \} \) . It maps each formula \( A \) to the formula \( {A}^{\mathrm{S}} = \neg {A}_{\mathrm{S}} \) , so \( {A}_{\mathrm{S}} \) is supposed to represent an intuitionistic version of the negation of \( A \) . The map from \( A \) to \( {A}_{\mathrm{S}} \) is defined recursively as follows, where in the first clause \( A \) is assumed to be atomic:

\[
{A}_{\mathrm{S}} = \neg A
\]

\[
{\left( \neg A\right) }_{\mathrm{S}} = \neg {A}_{\mathrm{S}}
\]

\[
{\left( A \land  B\right) }_{\mathrm{S}} = {A}_{\mathrm{S}} \vee  {B}_{\mathrm{S}}
\]

\[
{\left( A \vee  B\right) }_{\mathrm{S}} = {A}_{\mathrm{S}} \land  {B}_{\mathrm{S}}
\]

\[
{\left( \forall xA\right) }_{\mathrm{S}} = \exists x{A}_{\mathrm{S}}
\]

\[
{\left( \exists xA\right) }_{\mathrm{S}} = \neg \exists x\neg {A}_{\mathrm{S}}
\]

The translation \( A \mapsto  {A}^{\mathrm{S}} \) is parsimonious about adding negations: it adds one at the beginning of a formula, one in front of every atomic subformula, and two for every existential quantifier.

Theorem 4.6.7. Minimal logic proves \( {A}^{\mathrm{S}} \leftrightarrow  {A}^{\mathrm{N}} \) .

Proof Both translations are unchanged if we replace every existential quantifier by the classical equivalent in terms of \( \neg \) and \( \forall \) , so we can assume that there are no existential quantifiers in \( A \) . The cases where \( A \) is atomic or a negation are immediate. For \( \vee \) , we have

\[
{\left( A \vee  B\right) }^{\mathrm{S}} = \neg \left( {{A}_{\mathrm{S}} \land  {B}_{\mathrm{S}}}\right)  \equiv  \neg \left( {\neg \neg {A}_{\mathrm{S}}\land \neg \neg {B}_{\mathrm{S}}}\right)  \equiv  \neg \left( {\neg {A}^{\mathrm{N}}\land \neg {B}^{\mathrm{N}}}\right)  = {\left( A \vee  B\right) }^{\mathrm{N}},
\]

using the inductive hypothesis for \( A \) and \( B \) . For \( \land \) , we have

\[
{\left( A \land  B\right) }^{\mathrm{S}} = \neg \left( {{A}_{\mathrm{S}} \vee  {B}_{\mathrm{S}}}\right)  \equiv  \neg {A}_{\mathrm{S}} \land  \neg {B}_{\mathrm{S}} \equiv  {A}^{\mathrm{N}} \land  {B}^{\mathrm{N}} = {\left( A \land  B\right) }^{\mathrm{N}}.
\]

For \( \forall \) , we have

\[
{\left( \forall xA\right) }^{\mathrm{S}} = \neg \exists x{A}_{\mathrm{S}} \equiv  \forall x\neg {A}_{\mathrm{S}} \equiv  \forall x{A}^{\mathrm{N}} = {\left( \forall xA\right) }^{\mathrm{N}}.
\]

If we put \( A \) in negation normal form first, we can be even more parsimonious with negations. Define \( {A}^{\mathrm{M}} = \neg {A}_{\mathrm{M}} \) , where \( {A}_{\mathrm{M}} \) is defined recursively as follows, with \( A \) in the first two clauses atomic:

\[
{A}_{\mathrm{M}} = \neg A
\]

\[
{\left( \neg A\right) }_{\mathrm{M}} = A
\]

\[
{\left( A \land  B\right) }_{\mathrm{M}} = {A}_{\mathrm{M}} \vee  {B}_{\mathrm{M}}
\]

\[
{\left( A \vee  B\right) }_{\mathrm{M}} = {A}_{\mathrm{M}} \land  {B}_{\mathrm{M}}
\]

\[
{\left( \forall xA\right) }_{\mathrm{M}} = \exists x{A}_{\mathrm{M}}
\]

\[
{\left( \exists xA\right) }_{\mathrm{M}} = \neg \exists x{\left(  \sim  A\right) }_{\mathrm{M}}.
\]

Theorem 4.6.8. Minimal logic proves \( {A}^{\mathrm{M}} \leftrightarrow  {A}^{\mathrm{N}} \) .

The proof is left as an exercise. Of course, Theorems 4.6.7 and 4.6.8 imply that Theorem 4.6.3 holds with the \( N \) -translation replaced by the \( S \) -translation or \( M \) -translation.

For propositional logic, Theorem 2.7.11 shows that for any propositional formula \( A \) in negation normal form, \( {A}^{\mathrm{N}} \) is equivalent to \( \neg \left( { \sim  A}\right) \) in minimal logic. In first-order logic, the first implies the second, but in general the second is weaker. Nonetheless, we can still prove the following first-order variant of Corollary 2.7.12.

Theorem 4.6.9. Let \( A,{B}_{0},\ldots ,{B}_{n - 1} \) be in negation normal form. If \( \left\{  {{B}_{0},\ldots ,{B}_{n - 1}}\right\}  { \vdash  }_{\mathrm{C}}A \) then \( \left\{  {{B}_{0},\ldots ,{B}_{n - 1}}\right\}  { \vdash  }_{\mathrm{M}}\neg \left( { \sim  A}\right) \) .

To prove this, we need the following lemma, which can be proved using a straightforward induction on formulas:

Lemma 4.6.10. If \( A \) is in negation normal form, then \( { \vdash  }_{\mathrm{M}}A \rightarrow  {A}^{\mathrm{N}} \) .

If we replace by \( A \) by \( \sim  A \) in the statement of the lemma and take the contrapositive of the formula in the conclusion, we have that \( \neg {\left(  \sim  A\right) }^{\mathrm{N}} \rightarrow  \neg \left( { \sim  A}\right) \) is provable in minimal logic. The fact that \( \sim  A \) is equivalent to \( \neg A \) in classical logic implies that \( \neg {\left(  \sim  A\right) }^{\mathrm{N}} \) is equivalent to \( {A}^{\mathrm{N}} \) in minimal logic, which shows that \( {A}^{\mathrm{N}} \) implies \( \neg \left( { \sim  A}\right) \) in minimal logic. We can now prove Theorem 4.6.9.

Proof If \( \left\{  {{B}_{0},\ldots ,{B}_{n - 1}}\right\}  { \vdash  }_{\mathrm{C}}A \) then \( \left\{  {{B}_{0},\ldots ,{B}_{n - 1}, \sim  A}\right\}  { \vdash  }_{\mathrm{C}} \bot \) . By Theorem 2.7.6, we have \( \left\{  {{B}_{0}^{\mathrm{N}},\ldots ,{B}_{n - 1}^{\mathrm{N}},{\left(  \sim  A\right) }^{\mathrm{N}}}\right\}  { \vdash  }_{\mathrm{M}} \bot \) . We then have \( \left\{  {{B}_{0},\ldots ,{B}_{n - 1}, \sim  A}\right\}  { \vdash  }_{\mathrm{M}} \bot \) by Lemma 4.6.10, and hence \( \left\{  {{B}_{0},\ldots ,{B}_{n - 1}}\right\}  { \vdash  }_{\mathrm{M}}\neg \left( { \sim  A}\right) \) .

If we take \( A \) to be \( \exists x\neg P\left( x\right) \) for a predicate symbol \( P \) , then \( \neg \left( { \sim  A}\right)  \rightarrow  {A}^{\mathrm{N}} \) is \( \neg \forall {xP}\left( x\right)  \rightarrow \; \neg \forall x\neg \neg P\left( x\right) \) . This is, in turn, equivalent to \( \forall x\neg \neg P\left( x\right)  \rightarrow  \neg \neg \forall {xP}\left( x\right) \) in minimal logic. This is the principle of double-negation shift alluded to above, which shows that \( \neg \left( { \sim  A}\right) \) is strictly weaker than \( {A}^{\mathrm{N}} \) in intuitionistic logic. As a result, the translation \( A \mapsto  \neg \left( { \sim  A}\right) \) from classical to minimal logic is not modular: Exercise 4.6.6 asks you to show that from \( \neg \left( { \sim  {\left( A \rightarrow  B\right) }^{\text{ nnf }}}\right) \) and \( \neg \left( { \sim  {A}^{\text{ nnf }}}\right) \) it is, in general, not possible to conclude \( \neg \left( { \sim  {B}^{\text{ nnf }}}\right) \) intuitionistically.

### 4.7 Definite Descriptions

Suppose that, working with a formal language designed to reason about the natural numbers, we say what it means for \( z \) to be a greatest common divisor of \( x \) and \( y \) . Suppose we then prove that every pair of natural numbers \( x \) and \( y \) has a unique greatest common divisor. (Let us suppose that our definition specifies a value even in the case where \( x \) and \( y \) are both 0 .) From that moment on we would be justified in referring to the greatest common divisor of \( x \) and \( y \) . Such a specification of the greatest common divisor is an example of what is known as a definite description, and the goal of this section is to explain how to use first-order logic to interpret the corresponding use of the word "the."

The phrase "the \( x \) such that \( A\left( x\right) \) " presupposes that there is a unique \( x \) satisfying \( A \) . The following definition formalizes the notion of unique existence:

Definition 4.7.1. If \( A\left( x\right) \) is any formula, the expression \( \exists !{xA}\left( x\right) \) , read there is a unique \( x \) such that \( A\left( x\right) \) , is the formula \( \exists x\left( {A\left( x\right) \land \forall y\left( {A\left( y\right)  \rightarrow  y = x}\right) }\right) \) .

The exercises provide some equivalent formulations. From the assumption \( \exists !{xA} \) we can show that, for any formula \( B \) , the statements \( \exists x\left( {A \land  B}\right) \) and \( \forall x\left( {A \rightarrow  B}\right) \) are equivalent. Assuming \( \exists !{xA} \) , either can be taken to represent the statement that \( B \) holds of the \( x \) satisfying \( A \) .

In general, \( A \) can have variables other than \( x \) , in which case, the phrase "the \( x \) such that \( A \) " describes a function of these parameters. Assuming we have shown \( \forall \overrightarrow{x}\exists !{yA}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) \) , we should be justified in introducing a new function symbol \( f\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \) to denote the unique \( y \) satisfying \( A \) .

The next theorem and its proof show how we can interpret the use of such a function in the original language. The process is delicate because the function \( f \) can be composed with other function symbols, but the method is a straightforward extension of our analysis of the word "the."

The idea of expanding a theory to one in a larger language without altering the consequences in the original language is so common that it warrants some additional terminology.

Definition 4.7.2. Let \( \Gamma \) be a set of sentences in a language, \( L \) , and let \( {\Gamma }^{\prime } \supseteq  \Gamma \) be a set of sentences in a larger language, \( {L}^{\prime } \) . Suppose that whenever \( B \) is a formula in \( L \) and \( {\Gamma }^{\prime } \vdash \; B \) in first-order logic for \( {L}^{\prime } \) , then \( \Gamma  \vdash  B \) in first-order logic for \( L \) . Then \( {\Gamma }^{\prime } \) is said to be a conservative extension of \( \Gamma \) .

Let \( L \) be a first-order language, \( f\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \) be a new function symbol, and \( {L}_{f} \) be the language \( L \) extended by \( f \) . Let \( A\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) \) be a formula in \( L \) with at most the free variables shown. Think of \( A\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) \) as a way of expressing \( f\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right)  = y \) without mentioning \( f \) explicitly. Using the terminology just introduced, the theorem we are after is the following:

Theorem 4.7.3. If \( \Gamma  \vdash  \forall \overrightarrow{x}\exists !{yA}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) \) , then \( \Gamma  \cup  \{ \forall \overrightarrow{x}A\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, f\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) }\right) \} \) is a conservative extension of \( \Gamma \) .

The theorem holds for classical, intuitionistic, and minimal logic.

We will prove Theorem 4.7.3 by defining a translation that maps each formula \( B \) of \( {L}_{f} \) to a formula \( {B}^{ * } \) of \( L \) so that the following hold:

1. If \( B \) is in \( L \) , then \( {B}^{ * } \leftrightarrow  B \) is provable in first-order logic for \( {L}_{f} \) .

2. From hypothesis \( \forall \overrightarrow{x}, y\left( {A\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right)  \leftrightarrow  y = f\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) }\right) \) , we can prove \( {B}^{ * } \leftrightarrow  B \) for every \( B \) in \( {L}_{f} \) .

3. If \( \Gamma \) and \( B \) are formula of \( {L}_{f} \) and \( \Gamma  \cup  \{ \forall \overrightarrow{x}A\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, f\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) }\right) \} \) proves \( B \) in first-order logic for \( {L}_{f} \) , then \( {\Gamma }^{ * } \cup  \{ \forall \overrightarrow{x}\exists !{yA}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) \} \) proves \( {B}^{ * } \) in first-order logic for \( L \) .

In other words, the map \( B \mapsto  {B}^{ * } \) translates statements in the language with \( f \) to statements in which references to \( f \) are replaced by the description \( A\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) \) . All three claims will hold whether provability is interpreted in terms of classical, intuitionistic, or minimal logic. The first claim asserts that the translation leaves formulas in \( L \) essentially unchanged. The second guarantees that the translation means what we think it means: assuming \( A \) describes \( f \) , \( {B}^{ * } \) and \( B \) say the same thing. The third claim is that the translation respects provability. To see that this suffices to prove Theorem 4.7.3, suppose that \( \Gamma \) proves \( \forall \overrightarrow{x}\exists !{yA}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) \) , and suppose \( \Gamma  \cup  \{ \forall \overrightarrow{x}A\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, f\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) }\right) \} \) proves some formula \( B \) in \( L \) . By the first and third properties, we have that \( \Gamma \) together with \( \forall \overrightarrow{x}\exists !{yA}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) \) proves \( B \) . But since we are assuming that \( \Gamma \) proves \( \forall \overrightarrow{x}\exists !{yA}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) \) , we have that \( \Gamma \) proves \( B \) , as required.

Before defining the translation of formulas, we will define a translation of terms. Specifically, we will assign to each term \( t\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \) in the language \( {L}_{f} \) with the free variables shown a formula \( {C}_{t}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) \) in the language of \( L \) that represents the statement \( y = t\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \) without mentioning \( f \) . (Throughout, I will assume \( y \) is a variable different from all the \( x \) s.) The assignment is defined by induction on terms of \( {L}_{f} \) :

- For each variable \( x,{C}_{x}\left( {x, y}\right) \) is the formula \( y = x \) .

- If \( t \) is a term of the form \( f\left( {{t}_{0}\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) ,\ldots ,{t}_{k - 1}\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) }\right) \) , where \( f \) is the new function symbol, then \( {C}_{t}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) \) is the formula

\[
\exists {z}_{0},\ldots ,{z}_{k - 1}\left( {{C}_{{t}_{0}}\left( {\overrightarrow{x},{z}_{0}}\right)  \land  \cdots  \land  {C}_{{t}_{k - 1}}\left( {\overrightarrow{x},{z}_{k - 1}}\right)  \land  A\left( {\overrightarrow{z}, y}\right) }\right) ,
\]

where \( {z}_{0},\ldots ,{z}_{k - 1} \) are variables different from \( \overrightarrow{x} \) and \( y \) . (I have assumed that each \( {t}_{i} \) has the same parameters \( \overrightarrow{x} \) only for notational simplicity; otherwise, only the variables among \( \overrightarrow{x} \) occurring in \( {t}_{i} \) will occur in \( {C}_{{t}_{i}} \) .)

- If \( t \) is a term of the form \( g\left( {{t}_{0}\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) ,\ldots ,{t}_{m - 1}\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) }\right) \) for some function symbol \( g \) of \( L \) , then \( {C}_{t}\left( {\overrightarrow{w}, y}\right) \) is the formula

\[
\exists {z}_{0},\ldots ,{z}_{m - 1}\left( {{C}_{{t}_{0}}\left( {\overrightarrow{x},{z}_{0}}\right)  \land  \cdots  \land  {C}_{{t}_{k - 1}}\left( {\overrightarrow{x},{z}_{m - 1}}\right)  \land  y = g\left( \overrightarrow{z}\right) }\right) \text{ . }
\]

By induction on terms, it is straightforward to show the following:

1. For every term \( t\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \) of \( L \) , minimal logic proves \( \forall \overrightarrow{x}\left( {{C}_{t}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right)  \leftrightarrow  y = t\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) }\right) \) .

2. For every term \( t\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \) of \( {L}_{f} \) , minimal logic with \( \forall \overrightarrow{x}, y\left( {A\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right)  \leftrightarrow  y = f\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) }\right) \) proves \( \forall \overrightarrow{x}\left( {{C}_{t}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right)  \leftrightarrow  y = t\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) }\right) . \)

3. For every term \( t\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \) of \( {L}_{f} \) , minimal logic together with \( \forall \overrightarrow{x}\exists !{yA}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) \) proves \( \forall \overrightarrow{x}\exists !y{C}_{t}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) \) .

We also need to know that the translation respects substitution. Suppose \( s\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \) and \( t\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, w}\right) \) are terms with the free variables shown. Then assuming \( w = s\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \) , saying \( y = t\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, w}\right) \) is equivalent to saying \( y = t\left\lbrack  {s/w}\right\rbrack  \left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \) . Modulo our translation, this amounts to the following:

\[
{C}_{s}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, w}\right)  \rightarrow  \left( {{C}_{t}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, w, y}\right)  \leftrightarrow  {C}_{t\left\lbrack  {s/w}\right\rbrack  }\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) }\right) . \tag{4.1}
\]

We can show that this is provable in minimal logic from \( \forall \overrightarrow{x}\exists !{yA}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) \) using a straightforward induction on \( t \) . In the base case where \( t \) is a variable other than \( w \) , there is nothing to do. If \( t \) is the variable \( w \) , we need to show

\[
{C}_{s}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, w}\right)  \rightarrow  \left( {y = w \leftrightarrow  {C}_{s}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) }\right) .
\]

Assuming \( {C}_{s}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, w}\right) \) , the forward implication is immediate, and the reverse implication follows from the third claim listed above. In the induction step, where \( t \) is a composite term, it suffices to unwrap the definition of \( {C}_{t} \) and the definition of \( t\left\lbrack  {s/x}\right\rbrack \) and make use of the inductive hypothesis and claim 3.

Now that we can describe terms of \( {L}_{f} \) in the language of \( L \) , we can define a translation mapping formulas \( B \) of \( {L}_{f} \) to formulas \( {B}^{ * } \) of \( L \) , recursively, as follows:

- If \( s \) and \( t \) are terms of \( {L}_{f},{\left( s\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right)  = t\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \right) }^{ * } \) is the formula \( \exists y\left( {{C}_{s}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right)  \land  {C}_{t}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) }\right) \) .

- If \( R \) is an \( \ell \) -ary relation symbol of \( {L}_{f} \) and \( {t}_{0}\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) ,\ldots ,{t}_{\ell  - 1}\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \) are terms of \( {L}_{f} \) , then \( {\left( R\left( {t}_{0},\ldots ,{t}_{\ell  - 1}\right) \right) }^{ * } \) is the formula

\[
\exists {z}_{0},\ldots ,{z}_{\ell  - 1}\left( {{C}_{{t}_{0}}\left( {\overrightarrow{x},{z}_{0}}\right)  \land  \cdots  \land  {C}_{{t}_{\ell  - 1}}\left( {\overrightarrow{x},{z}_{\ell  - 1}}\right)  \land  R\left( {{z}_{0},\ldots ,{z}_{\ell  - 1}}\right) }\right) .
\]

- \( {\left( B \circ  C\right) }^{ * } = {B}^{ * } \circ  {C}^{ * } \) for any propositional connective \( \circ \) , and \( { \bot  }^{ * } =  \bot \) .

- \( {\left( \forall xB\right) }^{ * } = \forall x{B}^{ * } \) and \( {\left( \exists xB\right) }^{ * } = \exists x{B}^{ * } \) .

It is then straightforward to show the desired properties 1 and 2 of the translation of formulas from the corresponding properties of the translation of terms. Once again, we need to know that the translation behaves well with respect to substitution, namely, that for any formula \( B\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \) and term \( t\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \) , from \( \forall \overrightarrow{x}\exists !{yA}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) \) minimal logic can prove

\[
{C}_{t}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, w}\right)  \rightarrow  \left( {{B}^{ * }\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, w}\right)  \leftrightarrow  B{\left( \overrightarrow{x}, t\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \right) }^{ * }}\right) . \tag{4.2}
\]

This is proved by induction on \( B \) , using (4.1) in the base cases.

All we have left to do is prove property 3, namely, that if \( \Gamma \) and \( B \) are in the language \( {L}_{f} \) and \( \Gamma  \cup  \{ \forall \overrightarrow{x}A\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, f\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) }\right) \} \) proves \( B \) , then \( {\Gamma }^{ * } \cup  \{ \forall \overrightarrow{x}\exists !{yA}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) \} \) proves \( {B}^{ * } \) in \( L \) . First, we prove the claim in the case where \( \forall \overrightarrow{x}A\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, f\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) }\right) \) is omitted from the hypotheses in the assumption. In that case, we proceed by induction on derivations in natural deduction. The case for the assumption rule is immediate, and because the translation commutes with propositional connectives, the propositional rules are also easily handled. Handling the reflexivity, symmetry, and transitivity of equality is straightforward. We can take the remaining equality rule to be "from \( s\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right)  = t\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \) and \( B\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, s\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) }\right) \) conclude \( B\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, t\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) }\right) \) ." The hypothesis translates to \( \exists w\left( {{C}_{s}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, w}\right)  \land  {C}_{t}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, w}\right) }\right) \) and, for that \( w,\left( {4.2}\right) \) implies that \( B{\left( \overrightarrow{x}, s\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \right) }^{ * } \) and \( B{\left( \overrightarrow{x}, t\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \right) }^{ * } \) are both equivalent to \( {B}^{ * }\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, w}\right) \) .

The introduction rule for the universal quantifier and the elimination rule for the existential quantifier are easily handled. Suppose the last inference is an instance of the elimination rule for the universal quantifier, yielding \( B\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, t\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) }\right) \) from \( \forall {wB}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, w}\right) \) . The hypothesis translates to \( \forall w{B}^{ * }\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, w}\right) \) . We can also prove \( \exists !w{C}_{t}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, w}\right) \) , and (4.2) implies that, for that \( w \) , the conclusion is equivalent to \( {B}^{ * }\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, w}\right) \) . The elimination rule for the existential quantifier is handled similarly.

To handle the hypothesis \( \forall \overrightarrow{x}A\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, f\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) }\right) \) , first replace this by the equivalent formula \( \forall \overrightarrow{x}, y\left( {f\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right)  = y \rightarrow  A\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) }\right) \) . By the previous argument, \( {B}^{ * } \) is provable from \( {\Gamma }^{ * } \) together with the translation of this hypothesis. But since \( A \) is in \( L \) , the latter is equivalent to \( \forall \overrightarrow{x}, y\left( {A\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right)  \rightarrow  A\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) }\right) \) , which is provable outright.

To sum up, we have the following theorem:

Theorem 4.7.4. Let \( L \) be a first-order language, let \( A\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) \) be a formula of \( L \) with the free variables shown, let \( \Gamma , B \) be formulas of \( L \) , let \( f\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \) be a new function symbol, and let \( {L}_{f} \) denote the first-order language obtained by adding \( f \) to \( L \) . Suppose \( \Gamma  \cup  \{ \forall \overrightarrow{x}A\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, f\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) }\right) \} \) proves \( B \) in classical, intuitionistic, or minimal logic for \( {L}_{f} \) . Then \( \Gamma  \cup  \{ \forall \overrightarrow{x}\exists !{yA}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) \} \) proves \( B \) in the same system for \( L \) .

In particular, if \( \Gamma \) proves \( \forall \overrightarrow{x}\exists !{yA}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) \) then \( \Gamma \) proves \( B \) , and Theorem 4.7.3 follows.

I will refer to Theorems 4.7.3 and 4.7.4 as the elimination of definite descriptions. The process can be repeated, allowing us to conservatively extend our axiomatic theory with any number of function definitions. Similarly, we can conservatively extend a theory with a new relation symbol, \( R\left( {{x}_{0},\ldots ,{x}_{k - 1}}\right) \) , with defining axiom \( \forall \overrightarrow{x}\left( {R\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right)  \leftrightarrow  A\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) }\right) \) . Such definitions are more readily eliminated by replacing \( R \) by \( A \) everywhere. If \( T \) is any theory and \( {T}^{\prime } \) is obtained from \( T \) by any number of extensions of these two kinds, \( {T}^{\prime } \) is said to be a definitional extension of \( T \) .

When defining an axiomatic theory, it is often preferable to keep the number of primitives to a minimum, making it easier to study the theory and its metamathematical properties. When reasoning about what such a theory can do, however, it is useful to have a more expressive language. The use of definitional extensions is a way of having one's cake and eating it too.

The methods we have just developed also give us a way of eliminating function symbols in favor of relation symbols in a first-order language. Let \( {L}^{\prime } \) be a language with function symbols \( {f}_{0},\ldots ,{f}_{n - 1} \) . For each \( k \) -ary function symbol \( {f}_{i}\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \) , choose a \( \left( {k + 1}\right) \) -ary relation symbol \( {F}_{i}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) \) and let \( L \) be the language with each \( {f}_{i} \) replaced by \( {F}_{i} \) . Let \( \Pi \) be the set of axioms \( \forall \overrightarrow{x}\exists !y{F}_{i}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) \) . Then, for each \( i,{f}_{i} \) can be viewed as the function defined by \( {F}_{i} \) . Our translation takes any formula \( A \) in the language of \( {L}^{\prime } \) to a formula \( \widehat{A} \) in the language of \( L \) .

Proposition 4.7.5. Let \( L,{L}^{\prime },\Pi \) and the map \( A \mapsto  \widehat{A} \) be as above. Then \( A \) is provable in \( {L}^{\prime } \) if and only if \( \widehat{A} \) is provable from \( \Pi \) in \( L \) .

Proof Suppose \( A \) is provable in \( {L}^{\prime } \) . Then we also have, trivially, that \( A \) is provable from the set of hypotheses \( \left\{  {\ldots ,\forall \overrightarrow{y}{F}_{i}\left( {\overrightarrow{y},{f}_{i}\left( \overrightarrow{y}\right) }\right) ,\ldots }\right\} \) in \( L \cup  {L}^{\prime } \) . Viewing the functions \( {f}_{i} \) as witnessing the definite descriptions afforded by \( {F}_{i} \) and applying Theorem 4.7.4, we have that \( \widehat{A} \) is provable from \( \Pi \) in \( L \) .

Conversely, translate formulas \( B \) of \( {L}^{\prime } \) to formulas \( {B}^{ * } \) of \( L \) by replacing every atomic formula \( {F}_{i}\left( {\overrightarrow{s}, t}\right) \) by \( {f}_{i}\left( \overrightarrow{s}\right)  = t \) . Under this translation, every sentence in \( \Pi \) translates to an assertion of the form \( \forall \overrightarrow{x}\exists !y\left( {{f}_{i}\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right)  = y}\right) \) , which is provable in \( {L}^{\prime } \) . As a result, if \( B \) is provable from \( \Pi \) in \( L \) , then \( {B}^{ * } \) is provable in \( {L}^{\prime } \) . In particular, if \( \widehat{A} \) is provable from \( \Pi \) in \( L \) , then \( {\left( \widehat{A}\right) }^{ * } \) is provable in \( {L}^{\prime } \) . But it is not hard to show that for every formula \( C \) in \( {L}^{\prime },{\left( \widehat{C}\right) }^{ * } \leftrightarrow  C \) is provable in \( {L}^{\prime } \) . So \( A \) is provable in \( {L}^{\prime } \) .

What happens if, in Theorems 4.7.3 and 4.7.4, we drop uniqueness by replacing \( \forall \overrightarrow{x}\exists !{yA}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) \) by \( \forall \overrightarrow{x}\exists {yA}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) \) ? Assuming the weaker hypothesis, we can think of \( A\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) \) as providing an indefinite description of an element \( y \) depending on \( \overrightarrow{x} \) . A function \( f \) satisfying \( \forall \overrightarrow{x}A\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, f\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) }\right) \) is guaranteed to return some \( y \) satisfying \( A\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) \) , but in some cases more than one value of \( y \) may work. Such a function may therefore have to choose among the possible values of \( y \) .

We will see Chapter 7 that, somewhat surprisingly, adding such choice functions also yields a conservative extension of classical first-order logic. It does not yield a conservative extension of intuitionistic or minimal logic, but in those cases, we will find a simple axiomatization of the new consequences that are obtained.

### 4.8 Sorts and Undefined Terms

In this section we will consider two variations on first-order logic. Both increase the expressiveness of the language, but they can be interpreted straightforwardly using ordinary first-order logic.

As we have described first-order logic, there is only one universe of objects, and variables and quantifiers range over the entire universe. For many purposes, it is useful to have different sorts of objects in the universe, with each variable and quantifier ranging over objects of a particular sort. For Euclidean geometry, we might want to have a universe of points, \( p, q, r,\ldots \) and a universe of lines, \( l, m, n,\ldots \) , with a relation \( \operatorname{on}\left( {p, l}\right) \) that relates points and lines. The statement "two distinct points determine a line" would then be represented as

\[
\forall p, q\left( {p \neq  q \rightarrow  \exists !l\left( {\operatorname{on}\left( {p, l}\right)  \land  \operatorname{on}\left( {q, l}\right) }\right) }\right) .
\]

Here the quantifiers and variables are assumed to range over the appropriate sorts.

It is straightforward to modify the description of first-order logic to accommodate this. A many-sorted language comes with a set of sorts, and it specifies the sorts of the arguments to each function and relation symbol and the sort of each function symbol's output. In particular, there is a different equality symbol for each sort. Terms are defined as before, except that there is a stock of variables for each sort, and the term-forming operations are required to conform to the sort specifications in the language. Thus, each term inherits a sort, and the terms in an atomic formula \( R\left( {{t}_{0},\ldots ,{t}_{n - 1}}\right) \) are required to conform to specification of \( R \) ’s expected arguments. The quantifier axioms and rules are essentially the same, except that the sorts of the relevant variables and terms have to match. For example, in the axiom \( A\left( t\right)  \rightarrow  \exists {xA}\left( x\right) \) , the variable \( x \) is assumed to have the same sort as the term \( t \) .

The resulting logic is easily translated into ordinary first-order logic by adding a new predicate \( S\left( x\right) \) for each basic sort \( S \) of the many-sorted language and assigning a distinct variable \( {x}^{S} \) of the first-order language to each sorted variable \( {x}^{S} \) of the many-sorted language. In ordinary first-order logic, we can define the relativized quantifiers \( {\forall }^{S} \) and \( {\exists }^{S} \) by setting

\[
{\forall }^{S}{xA}\left( x\right)  \equiv  \forall x\left( {S\left( x\right)  \rightarrow  A\left( x\right) }\right)
\]

and

\[
{\exists }^{S}{xA}\left( x\right)  \equiv  \exists x\left( {S\left( x\right)  \land  A\left( x\right) }\right) .
\]

For each function symbol \( f\left( {{x}_{0}^{{S}_{0}},\ldots ,{x}_{k - 1}^{{S}_{k - 1}}}\right) \) of the many-sorted language expecting arguments of the sort shown and returning an argument of sort \( {S}_{k} \) , add an axiom to ordinary first-order logic,

\[
{S}_{0}\left( {x}^{{S}_{0}}\right)  \land  \cdots  \land  {S}_{k - 1}\left( {x}^{{S}_{k - 1}}\right)  \rightarrow  {S}_{k}\left( {f\left( {{x}_{0}^{{S}_{0}},\ldots ,{x}_{k - 1}^{{S}_{k - 1}}}\right) }\right) .
\]

Then translate each formula \( A\left( {{x}_{0}^{{S}_{0}},\ldots ,{x}_{k - 1}^{{S}_{k - 1}}}\right) \) of the many-sorted language with the free variables shown to the first-order formula \( {S}_{0}\left( {x}^{{S}_{0}}\right)  \land  \cdots  \land  {S}_{k - 1}\left( {x}^{{S}_{k - 1}}\right)  \rightarrow  \widehat{A}\left( {{x}_{0}^{{S}_{0}},\ldots ,{x}_{k - 1}^{{S}_{k - 1}}}\right) \) , where \( \widehat{A} \) is defined inductively as follows:

- \( \forall \widehat{{x}^{S}A} = {\forall }^{S}x\widehat{A} \) .

- \( \exists {x}^{S}A = {\exists }^{S}x\widehat{A} \) .

- The \( \widehat{} \) -translation fixes atomic statements and commutes with the other logical connectives.

Proposition 4.8.1. Let \( \Gamma \) be the additional axioms described above. Then for any formula \( A \) in the language of many-sorted first-order logic, the latter proves the formula \( A\left( {{x}_{0}^{{S}_{0}},\ldots }\right. \) , \( {x}_{k - 1}^{{S}_{k - 1}}) \) if and only if ordinary first-order logic, together with \( \Gamma \) , proves

\[
{S}_{0}\left( {x}_{0}^{{S}_{0}}\right)  \land  \cdots  \land  {S}_{k - 1}\left( {x}_{k - 1}^{{S}_{k - 1}}\right)  \rightarrow  \widehat{A}\left( {{x}_{0}^{{S}_{0}},\ldots ,{x}_{k - 1}^{{S}_{k - 1}}}\right) .
\]

Proof (sketch) The forward direction is obtained by a straightforward induction on proofs. The other direction is more subtle, because, a priori, an ordinary first-order proof in the extended language may not respect any of the sort conventions specified by the many-sorted language. One strategy is to restrict attention to formulas that are well sorted in the sense that all the quantifiers are relativized to sorts and it is possible to assign sorts to the variables in a manner that is consistent with those conventions. We can then appeal to the cut elimination theorem, discussed in Chapter 6, to argue that we can restrict attention to proofs in which there is an assignment of sorts to variables which makes all the formulas in the proof well-sorted. To each well-sorted formula \( A \) , we can then assign a formula \( \bar{A} \) which replaces each relativized quantifier with the corresponding many-sorted quantifier, and each atomic formula \( S\left( t\right) \) with an equality \( t = t \) of the appropriate sort. The first-order proof then translates back to a many-sorted proof of the -translation of the conclusion. Finally, by induction on formulas, we show that many-sorted logic can prove that this is equivalent to the original formula, \( A \) .

Exercise 4.8.3 below challenges you to find a proof that avoids the use of cut elimination. Alternatively, we can use a model-theoretic argument, using the methods developed in Chapter 7.

The other variation on first-order logic we will consider is designed to model logics where function symbols may be only partially defined. For example, in a theory of the real numbers it is natural to use a division symbol but take \( 1/0 \) to be an undefined term. In such a framework, variables are still assumed to range over elements of the universe, but since the function symbols denote partial functions, a term of the form \( f\left( {{t}_{0},\ldots ,{t}_{k - 1}}\right) \) may be undefined. To extend first-order logic to handle this, add a new predicate symbol, \( E \) , where \( E\left( t\right) \) is intended to express the fact that \( t \) exists, or \( t \) is defined, or \( t \) denotes. It is conventional to write \( t \downarrow \) for \( E\left( t\right) \) , and \( t \uparrow \) for \( \neg E\left( t\right) \) .

In a natural deduction formulation, the propositional axioms and rules remain the same, as do the introduction rule for the universal quantifier and elimination rule for the existential quantifier. But the elimination rule for the universal quantifier and the introduction rule for the existential quantifier are now expressed as follows, to reflect the fact that some terms may be undefined:

\[
\frac{\forall {xA}\left( x\right) \;t \downarrow  }{A\left( t\right) }\;\frac{A\left( t\right) \;t \downarrow  }{\exists {xA}\left( x\right) }
\]

The following axioms and rules reflect the fact that variables are assumed to range over (existing) elements of the domain, but functions may be partial:

\[
\overline{x \downarrow  }\;\overline{c \downarrow  }\;\frac{f\left( {{t}_{0},\ldots ,{t}_{n - 1}}\right)  \downarrow  }{{t}_{i} \downarrow  }\;\frac{R\left( {{t}_{0},\ldots ,{t}_{n - 1}}\right) }{{t}_{i} \downarrow  }
\]

The third rule implies that if any of \( {t}_{0},\ldots ,{t}_{n - 1} \) are undefined, then \( f\left( {{t}_{0},\ldots ,{t}_{n - 1}}\right) \) is undefined. The fourth rule says that any relation, including equality, can only hold of terms that are defined. If \( s \) and \( t \) are terms, the relation \( s \simeq  t \) of Kleene equality is defined to be \( s \downarrow   \vee  t \downarrow   \rightarrow  s = t \) . With the rule above, if \( s \simeq  t \) and either \( s \) or \( t \) is defined, then they are both defined and equal.

The equality axioms for reflexivity, symmetry, and transitivity are as before, as well as the congruence property for relations. For functions symbols, however, we replace the congruence property by the following:

\[
\begin{matrix} \overrightarrow{s} = \overrightarrow{t} \\  f\left( \overrightarrow{s}\right)  \simeq  f\left( \overrightarrow{t}\right)  \end{matrix}
\]

This reflects the fact that \( f\left( \overrightarrow{s}\right) \) and \( f\left( \overrightarrow{t}\right) \) may be undefined.

This extension of first-order logic is known as the logic of partial terms. It is not hard to interpret this system in ordinary first-order logic with a new predicate \( E \) . Because functions in ordinary first-order logic are total, we have to allow the first-order domain to contain elements that don’t exist and use the predicate \( E \) to specify the ones that do. We can explicitly restrict variables and quantifiers to range over elements that exist by translating \( A\left( {{x}_{0},\ldots ,{x}_{n - 1}}\right) \) to the formula \( E\left( {x}_{0}\right)  \land  E\left( {x}_{1}\right)  \land  \cdots  \land  E\left( {x}_{n - 1}\right)  \rightarrow  {A}^{ * }\left( {{x}_{0},\ldots ,{x}_{n - 1}}\right) \) , where \( {A}^{ * } \) is defined inductively as follows:

- \( {\left( R\left( {t}_{0},\ldots ,{t}_{n - 1}\right) \right) }^{ * } = E\left( {t}_{0}\right)  \land  \cdots  \land  E\left( {t}_{n - 1}\right)  \land  R\left( {{t}_{0},\ldots ,{t}_{n - 1}}\right) \) .

- \( {\left( \forall xA\left( x\right) \right) }^{ * } = \forall x\left( {E\left( x\right)  \rightarrow  A\left( x\right) }\right) \)

- \( {\left( \exists xA\left( x\right) \right) }^{ * } = \exists x\left( {E\left( x\right)  \land  A\left( x\right) }\right) \) .

- Otherwise, the *-translation commutes with logical connectives.

In addition, let \( \Gamma \) be the set of axioms

\[
\left\{  {\forall {x}_{0},\ldots ,{x}_{n - 1}\left( {E\left( {f\left( {{x}_{0},\ldots ,{x}_{n - 1}}\right) }\right)  \rightarrow  E\left( {x}_{0}\right)  \land  \cdots  \land  E\left( {x}_{n - 1}\right) }\right) }\right\}  .
\]

Proposition 4.8.2. Logic of partial terms proves \( A\left( {{x}_{0},\ldots ,{x}_{n - 1}}\right) \) if and only if \( \Gamma  \vdash  E\left( {x}_{0}\right)  \land \; E\left( {x}_{1}\right)  \land  \cdots  \land  E\left( {x}_{n - 1}\right)  \rightarrow  {A}^{ * }\left( {{x}_{0},\ldots ,{x}_{n - 1}}\right) \) in ordinary first-order logic.

Proof (sketch) Once again, it is straightforward to prove the forward direction by induction on derivations, while the backward direction is more subtle. As for Proposition 4.8.1, we can use a model-theoretic argument or appeal to the cut elimination theorem to restrict attention to proofs where all variables are assumed to satisfy the predicate \( E \) .

The logic of partial terms provides a natural way to introduce definite and indefinite descriptions axiomatically. It allows us to safely introduce a partial function symbol \( f\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \) to denote the unique value of \( y \) satisfying \( A\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) \) assuming there is one, with axioms \( \forall \overrightarrow{x}(f\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right)  \downarrow   \leftrightarrow \; \exists !{yA}\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) ) \) and \( \forall \overrightarrow{x}\left( {f\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right)  \downarrow   \rightarrow  A\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, f\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) }\right) }\right) \) . Writing \( f\left( \overrightarrow{t}\right) \) then presupposes that there is a unique \( y \) such that \( A\left( {\overrightarrow{t}, y}\right) \) holds; if there isn’t, the expression is simply undefined.

Axiomatizations of first-order logic The axiomatization of first-order logic given in Exercise 4.2.9 can be found in Shoenfield (2001). First-order logic as presented here presupposes a nonempty universe; for a variant of first-order logic that is also valid for the empty universe, see Lambek and Scott (1988).

Equational logic and universal algebra A structure for a language with no relation symbols is known as an algebra, and a class of algebras axiomatized by identities is known as a variety. Equational logic provides a complete proof system for establishing the identities that hold in a given variety. Birkhoff's theorem shows that a class of algebras is a variety if and only if it is closed under homomorphic images, subalgebras, and direct products; see Bergman (2015).

Automated reasoning Practical decision procedures for the quantifier-free fragment of classical logic usually rely on the congruence closure algorithm to handle equational reasoning. SMT solvers provide decision procedures for the quantifier-free fragment of classical first-order logic extended by combinations of theories that also have a decidable quantifier-free fragment, such as the ones discussion in Section 11.2. Here SMT stands for satisfiability modulo theories; see Harrison (2009), Bradley and Manna (2007), and Kroening and Strichman (2016). Since proving a formula \( A\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \) with free variables \( \overrightarrow{x} \) is the same as proving \( \forall \overrightarrow{x}A\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \) , a decision procedure for the quantifier-free consequences of a theory \( T \) is the same as a decision procedure for the universal consequences of \( T \) , also known as the universal fragment of \( T \) .

As noted in Section 4.4, the question as to whether an entailment holds in quantifier-free logic with the substitution rule, or, equivalently, the question as to whether a universal formula is a first-order consequence of some universal axioms, is generally undecidable. We will see in Section 7.5 that questions about provability in full classical logic can straightforwardly be reduced to this case. SMT solvers often allow universal axioms and use heuristics to instantiate the quantifiers, but they generally do not provide complete search procedures. That belongs to the realm of first-order theorem provers, for which see Robinson and Voronkov (2001) and the notes at the end of Chapters 6 and 7.

Ferreira and Oliva (2012) provides a thorough analysis of the variations; see also Troelstra and Schwichtenberg (2000). Theorem 4.6.7 is implicit in the presentation of the Dialectica interpretation in Shoenfield (2001).

Definition descriptions The analysis of definite descriptions is found in the landmark paper, Russell (1905). Russell and Whitehead used the notation \( {\iota x}.A \) to denote "the \( x \) satisfying \( A \) " in Principia Mathematica.

Logics for partial terms Feferman (1995) provides an overview of logics for partial terms. Beeson (1985), Troelstra and Schwichtenberg (2000), and Troelstra and van Dalen (1988) are also good references.

## 5 Semantics of First-Order Logic

In this chapter, we extend semantic notions that were introduced in Chapter 3 to first-order logic. Because the language of first-order logic is more complex than that of propositional logic, the semantics is more complex as well. Whereas propositional variables were interpreted as ranging over truth values, we now have to interpret first-order variables as ranging over objects in some domain of interpretation, and we have to explain the meaning of terms and formulas that contain such variables. But although there are more details to contend with, the ideas are quite similar.

### 5.1 Classical Logic

Fix a first-order language, \( L \) . Definition 1.1.2 says that an \( L \) -structure \( \mathfrak{M} \) consists of a nonempty set, \( \left| \mathfrak{M}\right| \) , called the universe of \( \mathfrak{M} \) , an interpretation of each function symbol \( f \) by a function \( {f}^{\mathfrak{M}} \) on \( \left| \mathfrak{M}\right| \) of the right arity, and an interpretation of each relation symbol \( R \) by a relation \( {R}^{\mathfrak{M}} \) on \( \left| \mathfrak{M}\right| \) of the right arity. Our goal here is to specify what it means for an \( L \) -structure to satisfy a sentence or set of sentences of \( L \) , in analogy to the sense in which a truth assignment can be said to satisfy a propositional formula or set of propositional formulas. When we think of the sentence or sentences as axioms, we generally to refer to an \( L \) -structure as a model.

Let \( \mathfrak{M} \) be an \( L \) -structure, let \( t \) be any term in \( L \) , and let \( \sigma \) be an assignment of elements of \( \left| \mathfrak{M}\right| \) to variables. Section 1.5 provides a recursive specification that assigns an element \( \llbracket t{\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma } \) of the universe of \( \mathfrak{M} \) . We interpret this as the denotation or evaluation of \( t \) in \( \mathfrak{M} \) under the assignment \( \sigma \) . In Section 1.5, we saw that the denotation only depends on the values that \( \sigma \) assigns to the variables of \( t \) . As a result, we could take \( \sigma \) to be an assignment to just those variables instead. Given a term \( t\left( {{x}_{0},\ldots ,{x}_{n - 1}}\right) \) with the free variables shown, we can use \( \llbracket t{\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M}} \) to denote the \( n \) -ary function defined by setting \( \llbracket t{\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M}}\left( {{a}_{0},\ldots ,{a}_{n - 1}}\right) \) equal the denotation of \( t \) under any assignment that maps each variable \( {x}_{i} \) to \( {a}_{i} \) .

In the classical, model-theoretic interpretation of first-order logic, a term therefore names an element of a structure, modulo an assignment of values to the free variables. In contrast, a formula is supposed to assert something that is either true or false, again modulo such an assignment. Our goal is therefore to specify a value \( \llbracket A{\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma } \) that is equal to either \( \top \) or \( \bot \) . Adapting the semantic notation introduced in Chapter 3, will write \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma }A \) for the statement \( \llbracket A{\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma } = \top \) . Read this as saying that \( A \) is true in \( \mathfrak{M} \) under assignment \( \sigma \) , that \( A \) holds in \( \mathfrak{M} \) under \( \sigma \) , or that \( \mathfrak{M} \) and \( \sigma \) satisfy \( A \) . If \( A \) has free variables \( {x}_{0},\ldots ,{x}_{n - 1} \) , we may write, suggestively, \( \mathfrak{M} \vDash  A\left( {{a}_{0},\ldots ,{a}_{n - 1}}\right) \) to say that \( A \) is true in \( \mathfrak{M} \) under any assignment that maps \( {x}_{0},\ldots ,{x}_{n - 1} \) to \( {a}_{0},\ldots ,{a}_{n - 1} \) .

Fixing a model \( \mathfrak{M} \) , the value \( \llbracket A{\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma } \) is defined by recursion on formulas \( A \) . To handle the quantifiers, it helps to adopt the following notation: if \( \sigma \) is an assignment of elements of \( \left| \mathfrak{M}\right| \) to variables, \( x \) is a variable, and \( a \) is an element of \( \left| \mathfrak{M}\right| \) , then \( \sigma \left\lbrack  {x \mapsto  a}\right\rbrack \) denotes the assignment that maps \( x \) to \( a \) and otherwise agrees with \( \sigma \) .

- \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma }t = {t}^{\prime } \) if and only if \( \llbracket t{\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma } = \llbracket {t}^{\prime }{\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma } \) .

- \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma }R\left( {{t}_{0},\ldots ,{t}_{n - 1}}\right) \) if and only if \( {R}^{\mathfrak{M}}\left( {\llbracket {t}_{0}{\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma },\ldots ,\llbracket {t}_{n - 1}{\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma }}\right) \) .

- \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma } \bot \) is always false.

- \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma }A \land  B \) if and only if \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma }A \) and \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma }B \) .

- \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma }A \vee  B \) if and only if \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma }A \) or \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma }B \) .

- \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma }A \rightarrow  B \) if and only if \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma }A \) or \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma }B \) .

- \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma }\forall {xA} \) if and only if for every \( a \in  \left| \mathfrak{M}\right| ,\mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma \left\lbrack  {x \mapsto  a}\right\rbrack  }A \) .

- \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma }\exists {xA} \) if and only if for some \( a \in  \left| \mathfrak{M}\right| ,\mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma \left\lbrack  {x \mapsto  a}\right\rbrack  }A \) .

In words, \( t = {t}^{\prime } \) holds in \( \mathfrak{M} \) under \( \sigma \) if and only if \( t \) and \( {t}^{\prime } \) denote the same element of the universe of \( \mathfrak{M} \) under \( \sigma \) . Similarly, \( R\left( {{t}_{0},\ldots ,{t}_{n - 1}}\right) \) holds in \( \mathfrak{M} \) under \( \sigma \) if and only if the interpretation of \( R \) holds of the elements denoted by \( {t}_{0},\ldots ,{t}_{n - 1} \) under \( \sigma \) . The clauses describing the evaluation of formulas under the propositional connectives are carried over from the propositional setting. A formula \( \forall {xA} \) holds in \( \mathfrak{M} \) under \( \sigma \) if and only if \( A \) holds no matter what element of the universe of \( \mathfrak{M} \) is assigned to \( x \) , and a formula \( \exists {xA} \) holds if and only if there is some assignment to \( x \) for which \( A \) holds in \( \mathfrak{M} \) . According to the dictates of Section 1.6, we need to specify a choice of variable with which to open the bound quantifiers in the recursion scheme, but we can show after the fact that the result does not depend on the particular choice. We can think of a formula \( A\left( {{x}_{0},\ldots ,{x}_{n - 1}}\right) \) with the free variables shown as defining an \( n \) -ary relation \( \llbracket A{\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M}} \) on the universe of \( \left| \mathfrak{M}\right| \) , whereby \( \llbracket A{\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M}}\left( {{a}_{0},\ldots ,{a}_{n - 1}}\right) \) means \( \mathfrak{M} \vDash  A\left( {{a}_{0},\ldots ,{a}_{n - 1}}\right) \) .

There is an alternative, equivalent approach to defining satisfiability of formulas in a model that avoids the need to carry around the assignment \( \sigma \) . If \( L \) is a language and \( \mathfrak{M} \) is a structure for \( L \) , let \( L\left( \mathfrak{M}\right) \) denote the language obtained by adding a new constant \( \bar{a} \) for each element \( a \in  \mathfrak{M} \) . We now restrict the evaluation to closed terms and sentences in the expanded language, and the clauses for the universal and existential quantifiers now read as follows:

- \( \mathfrak{M} \vDash  \forall {xA}\left( x\right) \) if and only if for every \( a \in  \left| \mathfrak{M}\right| ,\mathfrak{M} \vDash  A\left( \bar{a}\right) \) .

- \( \mathfrak{M} \vDash  \exists {xA}\left( x\right) \) if and only if for some \( a \in  \left| \mathfrak{M}\right| ,\mathfrak{M} \vDash  A\left( \bar{a}\right) \) .

Exercise 5.1.1 asks you to show that for any formula \( A\left( {{x}_{0},\ldots ,{x}_{n - 1}}\right) \) with the free variables shown, \( \mathfrak{M} \vDash  A\left( {{\bar{a}}_{0},\ldots ,{\bar{a}}_{n - 1}}\right) \) in the new sense if and only if \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma }A \) in the old sense for any \( \sigma \) such that \( \sigma \left( {x}_{i}\right)  = {a}_{i} \) for each \( i < n \) .

We will later have occasion to speak of a formula \( A\left( {\overrightarrow{x},\overrightarrow{a}}\right) \) with parameters from a model \( \mathfrak{M} \) . This can be understood as a formula of \( L\left( \mathfrak{M}\right) \) in which each new constant \( \bar{a} \) is interpreted as the corresponding element \( a \in  \left| \mathfrak{M}\right| \) , or, equivalently, a formula \( A\left( {\overrightarrow{x},\overrightarrow{y}}\right) \) with a distinguished tuple of variables \( \overrightarrow{y} \) and a specification \( \overrightarrow{a} \) of the elements of \( \left| \mathfrak{M}\right| \) that they are assigned to them.

Now that we have defined the notion of satisfiability, the remaining semantic notions carry over from Chapter 3.

Definition 5.1.1. Let \( L \) be a language, let \( A \) be a sentence of \( L \) , let \( \Gamma \) be a set of sentences of \( L \) , and let \( \mathfrak{M} \) be an \( L \) -structure.

- \( \mathfrak{M} \vDash  \Gamma \) means that \( \mathfrak{M} \vDash  B \) for every \( B \in  \Gamma \) . In this case, we say that \( \mathfrak{M} \) satisfies \( \Gamma \) , or that \( \mathfrak{M} \) is a model of \( \Gamma \) .

- \( A \) is satisfiable if it is true in some model, and similarly for \( \Gamma \) .

- \( A \) is valid, written \( \vDash  A \) , if it is true in every model.

- \( A \) is a logical consequence of \( \Gamma \) , written \( \Gamma  \vDash  A \) , if every model of \( \Gamma \) is also a model of \( A \) . In that case, we also say that \( A \) is a semantic consequence of \( \Gamma \) , that \( \Gamma \) logically entails \( A \) , or, more simply, that \( \Gamma \) entails \( A \) .

As shorthand, one often describes a structure by simply listing its components, starting with the universe. For example, \( \left( {\mathbb{N},0,1,+,\cdot , < }\right) \) is a structure for a language with two constant symbols, two binary function symbols, and one binary relation symbol, with the corresponding interpretations.

We will need to know that our semantic notions play well with substitution, in the following sense.

Proposition 5.1.2. Let \( s \) and \( t \) be terms of a language \( L \) , let \( A \) be a formula of \( L \) , let \( x \) be a variable, and let \( \mathfrak{M} \) be an L-structure. Let \( \sigma \) be an assignment of elements of \( \left| \mathfrak{M}\right| \) to variables and let \( a = \llbracket s{\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma } \) .

1. \( \llbracket t\left\lbrack  {s/x}\right\rbrack  {\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma } = \llbracket t{\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma \left\lbrack  {x \mapsto  a}\right\rbrack  } \)

2. \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma }A\left\lbrack  {s/x}\right\rbrack \) if and only if \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma \left\lbrack  {x \mapsto  a}\right\rbrack  }A \) .

In words, the first claim says that substituting \( s \) for \( x \) in a term \( t \) and then evaluating it in \( \mathfrak{M} \) has the same effect as evaluating \( t \) with \( x \) assigned to result of evaluating \( s \) . The second claim is the analogous statement for a formula \( A \) . The two claims can be proved by induction on terms and formulas, respectively.

We now turn to the task of proving the soundness and completeness of classical first-order logic with respect to the semantics just described. For the rest of this section, let us assume we have fixed a language, \( L \) .

Theorem 5.1.3 (soundness). For every set of sentences \( \Gamma \) and every sentence \( A \) , if \( \Gamma  \vdash  A \) , then \( \Gamma  \vDash  A \) .

Proof By induction on natural deduction derivations, we can show a more general claim: if \( \Gamma \) is a finite set of formulas and \( A \) is any formula, then for every assignment \( \sigma \) , if \( \Gamma  \vdash  A \) is derivable and \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma }\Gamma \) , then \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma }A \) . The propositional rules are handled just as for propositional logic. For the \( \forall \) introduction rule, suppose we derive \( \Gamma  \vdash  \forall {xA} \) from \( \Gamma  \vdash  A \) , and suppose \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma }\Gamma \) . To show \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma }\forall {xA} \) , let \( a \) be any element of \( \left| \mathfrak{M}\right| \) ; we need to show \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma \left\lbrack  {x \mapsto  a}\right\rbrack  }A \) . By the eigenvariable condition, \( x \) does not occur free in \( \Gamma \) , and since \( s \) and \( \sigma \left\lbrack  {x \mapsto  a}\right\rbrack \) agree on all the other variables, \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma \left\lbrack  {x \mapsto  a}\right\rbrack  }\Gamma \) . By the inductive hypothesis, we have \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma \left\lbrack  {x \mapsto  a}\right\rbrack  }A \) .

For the \( \forall \) elimination rule, suppose we derive \( \Gamma  \vdash  A\left\lbrack  {t/x}\right\rbrack \) from \( \Gamma  \vdash  \forall {xA} \) , and suppose \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma }\Gamma \) . By the inductive hypothesis, we have \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma }\forall {xA} \) . From the definition of the satisfaction relation, we have \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma \left\lbrack  {x \mapsto  \llbracket t{\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma }}\right\rbrack  }A \) . By Proposition 5.1.2, this is equivalent to \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma }A\left\lbrack  {t/x}\right\rbrack \) , as required.

The introduction and elimination rules for the existential quantifier, as well as the rules for equality, are left as an exercise.

Exercise 5.1.3 suggests an alternative approach to proving Theorem 5.1.3 that avoids the appeal to Proposition 5.1.2 by adopting a more general inductive claim: if \( \Gamma  \vdash  A \) , then for any tuple of variables \( \overrightarrow{x} \) , any tuple of terms \( \overrightarrow{t} \) , any model \( \mathfrak{M} \) , and any assignment \( \sigma \) , if \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma } \; \Gamma \left\lbrack  {\overrightarrow{t}/\overrightarrow{a}}\right\rbrack \) , then \( \mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma }A\left\lbrack  {\overrightarrow{t}/\overrightarrow{a}}\right\rbrack \) .

Theorem 5.1.4 (completeness). For every set of sentences \( \Gamma \) and every sentence \( A \) , if \( \Gamma  \vDash  A \) , then \( \Gamma  \vdash  A \) .

First we will prove completeness for first-order logic without equality, and then we will extend the proof to handle equality as well. As for propositional logic, we will say that a set of sentences \( \Gamma \) is consistent if \( \Gamma  \nvdash   \bot \) . And, as was the case with propositional logic, completeness is equivalent to the following:

Theorem 5.1.5. For every set of sentences \( \Gamma \) , if \( \Gamma \) is consistent, then it has a model.

In the case of propositional logic, we were required to come up with a truth assignment satisfying \( \Gamma \) . Now we need a model, that is, a universe and functions and relations defined on that universe. Since all we have to work with are the sentences in \( \Gamma \) , the only option available is to build the universe out of syntax. For example, we might take the universe to be the set of closed terms in the language and interpret each function symbol as the corresponding term-forming operation. One complication is that if there are no constant symbols in \( L \) , there won’t be any terms. So our first step is to expand \( \Gamma \) to a set of sentences in a larger language, \( {L}^{\prime } \) , to ensure that there are enough constants.

Definition 5.1.6. Let \( \Gamma \) be a set of sentences in a language \( L.\Gamma \) is a Henkin set if the following holds: for every sentence \( \exists {xA}\left( x\right) \) in \( L \) , there is a constant \( c \) such that \( \Gamma  \vdash  \exists {xA}\left( x\right)  \rightarrow  A\left( c\right) \) .

A constant \( c \) associated with the sentence \( \exists {xA}\left( x\right) \) in this way is called a Henkin constant.

Lemma 5.1.7. Let \( \Gamma \) be a set of sentences in a language \( L \) . Then there is a language \( {L}^{\prime } \) extending \( L \) and a set of sentences \( {\Gamma }^{\prime } \supseteq  \Gamma \) of \( {L}^{\prime } \) such that

- \( {\Gamma }^{\prime } \) is a Henkin set, and

- \( {\Gamma }^{\prime } \) is a conservative extension of \( \Gamma \) .

Recall from Definition 4.7.2 that saying that \( {\Gamma }^{\prime } \) is a conservative extension of \( \Gamma \) means that if \( {\Gamma }^{\prime } \vdash  B \) and \( B \) is a formula of \( L \) , then \( \Gamma  \vdash  B \) . To prove Lemma 5.1.7, we need the following lemma, which shows that we can conservatively add a single Henkin constant.

Lemma 5.1.8. Let \( \Gamma \) be a set of sentences in a language \( L \) , let \( \exists {xA}\left( x\right) \) be any sentence, and let \( c \) be a new constant. Suppose \( \Gamma  \cup  \{ \exists {xA}\left( x\right)  \rightarrow  A\left( c\right) \}  \vdash  B \) , where \( B \) is in \( L \) . Then \( \Gamma  \vdash  B \) .

Proof Suppose \( \Gamma  \cup  \{ \exists {xA}\left( x\right)  \rightarrow  A\left( c\right) \}  \vdash  B \) . By the deduction theorem,

\[
\Gamma  \vdash  \left( {\exists {xA}\left( x\right)  \rightarrow  A\left( c\right) }\right)  \rightarrow  B.
\]

By Proposition 4.2.1, we can replace \( c \) by a fresh variable, so we have

\[
\Gamma  \vdash  \forall y\left( {\left( {\exists {xA}\left( x\right)  \rightarrow  A\left( y\right) }\right)  \rightarrow  B}\right) .
\]

This last sentence is equivalent to \( \exists y\left( {\exists {xA}\left( x\right)  \rightarrow  A\left( y\right) }\right)  \rightarrow  B \) , and the antecedent is provable in classical logic, so \( \Gamma  \vdash  B \) .

Proof of Lemma 5.1.7 Let us focus on the case where \( L \) is a countable language. The argument can be adapted to uncountable languages using Zorn's lemma. (See Section A.3.)

Let \( {L}^{\prime } \) be \( L \) together with countably many new constants. Enumerate all sentences \( {C}_{0},{C}_{1} \) , \( {C}_{2},\ldots \) of \( {L}^{\prime } \) that are of the form \( \exists {xA}\left( x\right) \) . Inductively define a sequence of formulas \( {D}_{0},{D}_{1} \) , \( {D}_{2},\ldots \) so that each \( {D}_{i} \) is a Henkin axiom for \( {C}_{i} \) using a constant that does not occur in \( {D}_{0},\ldots ,{D}_{i - 1} \) . Let \( {\Gamma }^{\prime } = \Gamma  \cup  \left\{  {{D}_{0},{D}_{1},{D}_{2},\ldots }\right\} \) . Then every sentence \( \exists {xA}\left( x\right) \) in \( {L}^{\prime } \) has a Henkin axiom in \( {\Gamma }^{\prime } \) . By Lemma 5.1.8, for each \( i,\Gamma  \cup  \left\{  {{D}_{0},\ldots ,{D}_{i + 1}}\right\} \) is conservative over \( \Gamma  \cup  \left\{  {{D}_{0},\ldots ,{D}_{i}}\right\} \) , and hence, by induction, conservative over \( \Gamma \) . Since anything provable from \( {\Gamma }^{\prime } \) is provable from \( \Gamma  \cup  \left\{  {{D}_{0},\ldots ,{D}_{i}}\right\} \) for some \( i \) , this means that \( {\Gamma }^{\prime } \) is conservative over \( \Gamma \) .

The exercises below provide an alternative proof of this lemma. We can now prove Theorem 5.1.4, which asserts the completeness of our deductive systems for classical first-order logic, in the case where we do not include equality as a special relation symbol.

Proof of Theorem 5.1.4 for first-order logic without equality Let \( \Gamma \) be a consistent set of sentences in \( L \) . By Lemma 5.1.7, let \( {\Gamma }^{\prime } \) be a Henkin set in a larger language, \( {L}^{\prime } \) , such that \( {\Gamma }^{\prime } \) is conservative over \( \Gamma \) . In particular, this means that \( {\Gamma }^{\prime } \) is consistent, since conservativity implies that if \( {\Gamma }^{\prime } \) proves \( \bot \) then so does \( \Gamma \) . Any model \( \mathfrak{M} \) of \( {\Gamma }^{\prime } \) becomes a model of \( \Gamma \) is we ignore the interpretations of the extra constants, so it suffices to show that \( {\Gamma }^{\prime } \) has a model.

As in the proof of Lemma 3.1.13, extend \( {\Gamma }^{\prime } \) to a maximally consistent set \( \Delta \) in the same language. Notice that \( \Delta \) is still a Henkin set because the language has not changed. I claim that \( \Delta \) has all the following properties:

1. \( \Delta \) is deductively closed: if \( \Delta  \vdash  A \) , then \( A \in  \Delta \) .

2. \( \bot   \notin  \Delta \) .

3. \( A \land  B \in  \Delta \) if and only if \( A \in  \Delta \) and \( B \in  \Delta \) .

4. \( A \rightarrow  B \in  \Delta \) if and only if \( A \notin  \Delta \) or \( B \in  \Delta \) .

5. \( A \vee  B \in  \Delta \) if and only if \( A \in  \Delta \) or \( B \in  \Delta \) .

6. \( \exists {xA}\left( x\right) \) is in \( \Delta \) if and only if for some closed term \( t, A\left( t\right)  \in  \Delta \) .

7. \( \forall {xA}\left( x\right) \) is in \( \Delta \) if and only if for every closed term \( t, A\left( t\right)  \in  \Delta \) .

Claims 1-5 are handled just as in the propositional case. For claim 6, suppose \( \exists {xA}\left( x\right) \) is in \( \Delta \) . Since \( \Delta \) is a Henkin set, the sentence \( \exists {xA}\left( x\right)  \rightarrow  A\left( c\right) \) is in \( \Delta \) for some constant \( c \) . So \( \Delta  \vdash  A\left( c\right) \) , and hence \( A\left( c\right)  \in  \Delta \) . Conversely, suppose for some term \( t, A\left( t\right)  \in  \Delta \) . Then \( \Delta  \vdash  \exists {xA}\left( x\right) \) , and hence \( \exists {xA}\left( x\right)  \in  \Delta \) .

The proof of claim 7 is similar. The forward direction is immediate, and since \( \Delta \) is a Henkin set, for some constant \( c,\exists x\neg A\left( x\right)  \rightarrow  \neg A\left( c\right) \) is in \( \Delta \) . If \( A\left( t\right) \) is in \( \Delta \) for every closed term \( t \) , then \( A\left( c\right) \) is in \( \Delta \) . Together these imply that \( \neg \exists x\neg A\left( x\right) \) , which is equivalent to \( \forall {xA}\left( x\right) \) , is in \( \Delta \) .

Just as a maximally consistent set of propositional formulas enabled us to read off a satisfying truth assignment in Section 3.1, a maximally consistent Henkin set \( \Delta \) enables us to read off a satisfying model, \( \mathfrak{M} \) , as follows:

- \( \left| \mathfrak{M}\right|  = \left\{  {t \mid  t}\right. \) is a closed term of \( \left. {L}^{\prime }\right\} \) .

- For each \( k \) -ary function symbol \( f \) , if \( {t}_{0},\ldots ,{t}_{k - 1} \) are elements of \( \left| \mathfrak{M}\right| \) , we set \( {f}^{\mathfrak{M}}\left( {{t}_{0},\ldots }\right. \) , \( {t}_{k - 1} \) ) equal to the term \( f\left( {{t}_{0},\ldots ,{t}_{k - 1}}\right) \) .

- For each \( k \) -ary relation symbol \( R \) , if \( {t}_{0},\ldots ,{t}_{k - 1} \) are elements of \( \left| \mathfrak{M}\right| \) , we specify that \( {R}^{\mathfrak{M}}\left( {{t}_{0},\ldots ,{t}_{k - 1}}\right) \) holds if and only if \( R\left( {{t}_{0},\ldots ,{t}_{k - 1}}\right) \) is in \( \Delta \) .

The construction is entirely syntactic: the universe of the model consists of terms and the interpretation \( {f}^{\mathfrak{M}} \) of each function symbol \( f \) is an operation on terms.

By induction on terms, we have that if \( t\left( {{x}_{0},\ldots ,{x}_{k - 1}}\right) \) is any term in \( {L}^{\prime } \) with the variables shown and \( \sigma \) is any assignment, then

\[
\llbracket t{\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma } = t\left( {\sigma \left( {x}_{0}\right) ,\ldots ,\sigma \left( {x}_{k - 1}\right) }\right) .
\]

Similarly, using the properties of \( \Delta \) enumerated above, we have that if \( A\left( {{x}_{0},\ldots ,{x}_{k - 1}}\right) \) is any formula of \( {L}^{\prime } \) and \( \sigma \) is any assignment,

\[
\mathfrak{M}{ \vDash  }_{\sigma }A\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \; \Leftrightarrow  \;A\left( {\sigma \left( {x}_{0}\right) ,\ldots ,\sigma \left( {x}_{k - 1}\right) }\right)  \in  \Delta .
\]

In particular, since \( \Delta \) includes \( \Gamma \) , if we restrict \( \mathfrak{M} \) to interpret the symbols of \( L \) , we have a model of \( \Gamma \) .

Let \( \mathfrak{M} \) be any \( L \) -structure and let \( L\left( \mathfrak{M}\right) \) be the expanded language with a new constant, \( \bar{a} \) , for every element \( a \) of \( \mathfrak{M} \) . If \( A \) is a sentence in this expanded language, we can abuse notation slightly and write \( \mathfrak{M} \vDash  A \) when the expansion of \( \mathfrak{M} \) to \( {L}^{\prime } \) satisfies \( A \) . It is not hard to check that the set \( \{ A \mid  \mathfrak{M} \vDash  A\} \) is a maximally consistent Henkin set. Our proof of the completeness theorem shows that every maximally consistent Henkin set arises in this way.

An advantage to the approach we have followed is that it is modular: first, we add Henkin axioms, and then we extend to a maximally consistent set. An alternative approach is to add build a maximally consistent set and add suitable constants at the same time. In the countable case, let \( {L}^{\prime } \) , as above, be a language with countably many new constant symbols. Let \( {B}_{0},{B}_{1},{B}_{2},\ldots \) enumerate all sentences in the language, and let \( \exists x{C}_{0}\left( x\right) ,\exists x{C}_{1}\left( x\right) ,\ldots \) enumerate all sentences of the form \( \exists {xC}\left( x\right) \) . Then modify the construction in the proof of Lemma 3.1.13 to define a sequence \( \Gamma  = {\Gamma }_{0},{\Gamma }_{1},{\Gamma }_{2},\ldots \) so that at stage \( i + 1 \) , we

- add \( {B}_{i} \) to \( {\Gamma }_{i} \) , assuming the result is consistent, and

- if the resulting set proves \( \exists x{C}_{i}\left( x\right) \) , add \( {C}_{i}\left( c\right) \) , where \( c \) does not occur in any \( {B}_{j} \) or \( {C}_{j} \) for \( j < i \) .

The exercises below ask you to show that the set \( {\Gamma }_{\omega } = \mathop{\bigcup }\limits_{i}{\Gamma }_{i} \) satisfies the properties required in the previous proof.

All we have left to do is extend the proof of the completeness theorem to first-order logic with equality. Suppose a set of sentences \( \Gamma \) proves a formula \( A \) in that setting. Let \( \Delta \) be the set of axioms for equality described in Section 4.3. Then, treating the equality symbol as an ordinary binary relation symbol, \( \Gamma  \cup  \Delta \) proves \( A \) in first-order logic without equality. In particular, if \( \Gamma \) is consistent with respect to first-order logic with equality, then \( \Gamma  \cup  \Delta \) is consistent with respect to first-order logic without equality. We have already shown that \( \Gamma  \cup  \Delta \) has a model, \( \mathfrak{M} \) , in which the equality symbol is interpreted as an ordinary binary relation symbol. Let \( \sim \) denote the interpretation of equality in \( \mathfrak{M} \) . The fact that \( \mathfrak{M} \) satisfies \( \Delta \) means that \( \sim \) is reflexive, symmetric, and transitive. It is also a congruence with respect to every function and relation symbol. This means that if \( f \) is an \( n \) -ary function symbol, \( {a}_{0},\ldots ,{a}_{n - 1},{b}_{0},\ldots ,{b}_{n - 1} \) are elements of \( U \) , and \( {a}_{i} \sim  {b}_{i} \) for every \( i \) , then \( {f}^{\mathfrak{M}}\left( {{a}_{0},\ldots ,{a}_{n - 1}}\right)  \sim  {f}^{\mathfrak{M}}\left( {{b}_{0},\ldots ,{b}_{n - 1}}\right) \) , and similarly for relation symbols.

We could stop here: what we now know that if we allow equality to be interpreted by any such relation, we have a complete semantics for classical first-order logic with equality. But it would be nice to know that we can rely on the narrower semantics where the equality symbol is interpreted by the equality relation itself on the elements of the universe. For that purpose, quotient structure \( \mathfrak{M}/ \sim \) , defined in Section 1.1, has the properties we need. Recall that it is defined as follows:

- The universe of \( \mathfrak{M}/ \sim \) is the set of equivalence classes of elements of \( \left| \mathfrak{M}\right| \) , i.e. the set \( \{ \left\lbrack  a\right\rbrack  \left| {a \in  }\right| \mathfrak{M} \mid  \} \) , where \( \left\lbrack  a\right\rbrack \) denotes the equivalence class \( \{ b \in  \left| \mathfrak{M}\right|  \mid  b \sim  a\} \) .

- For each function symbol \( f \) of \( L \) , the interpretation \( {f}^{\mathfrak{M}/ \sim  } \) is defined by

\[
{f}^{\mathfrak{M}/ \sim  }\left( {\left\lbrack  {a}_{0}\right\rbrack  ,\ldots ,\left\lbrack  {a}_{n - 1}\right\rbrack  }\right)  = \left\lbrack  {{f}^{\mathfrak{M}}\left( {{a}_{0},\ldots ,{a}_{n - 1}}\right) }\right\rbrack  .
\]

- For each relation symbol \( R \) of \( L \) other than equality, the interpretation \( {R}^{\mathfrak{M}/ \sim  } \) is defined by

\[
{R}^{\mathfrak{M}/ \sim  }\left( {\left\lbrack  {a}_{0}\right\rbrack  ,\ldots ,\left\lbrack  {a}_{n - 1}\right\rbrack  }\right) \; \Leftrightarrow  \;{R}^{\mathfrak{M}}\left( {{a}_{0},\ldots ,{a}_{n - 1}}\right) .
\]

Equality in \( L \) is interpreted as equality on the universe of \( \mathfrak{M}/ \sim \) . The fact that \( \sim \) is an equivalence relation is needed to ensure that this model is well defined, which is to say, the definitions of the functions and relations on \( \mathfrak{M}/ \sim \) do not depend on the choice of representatives of the equivalence classes. We can show inductively that if \( t\left( {{x}_{0},\ldots ,{x}_{n - 1}}\right) \) is any term in the language of \( L \) with the variables shown and \( {a}_{0},\ldots ,{a}_{n - 1} \) are elements of \( \left| \mathfrak{M}\right| \) , then

\[
{\left\lbrack  t\left( \left\lbrack  {a}_{0}\right\rbrack  ,\ldots ,\left\lbrack  {a}_{n - 1}\right\rbrack  \right) \right\rbrack  }^{\mathfrak{M}/ \sim  } = \left\lbrack  {\left\lbrack  t\left( {a}_{0},\ldots ,{a}_{n - 1}\right) \right\rbrack  }^{\mathfrak{M}}\right\rbrack  . \tag{5.1}
\]

Furthermore, if \( A\left( {{x}_{0},\ldots ,{x}_{n - 1}}\right) \) is any formula of \( L \) with the free variables shown and \( {a}_{0},\ldots ,{a}_{n - 1} \) are elements of \( \left| \mathfrak{M}\right| \) , we have

\[
\mathfrak{M}/ \sim   \vDash  A\left( {\left\lbrack  {a}_{0}\right\rbrack  ,\ldots ,\left\lbrack  {a}_{n - 1}\right\rbrack  }\right) \; \Leftrightarrow  \;\mathfrak{M} \vDash  A\left( {{a}_{0},\ldots ,{a}_{n - 1}}\right) .
\]

Remember that, on the left, we are interpreting the equality symbol by equality in the model, whereas on the right, it is interpreted by the relation \( \sim \) . All the work in proving this equivalence is in the base case, for atomic formulas of the form \( R\left( {{t}_{0},\ldots ,{t}_{n - 1}}\right) \) and \( {t}_{1} = {t}_{2} \) . In these cases, the equivalence follows straightforwardly from the definition of \( \mathfrak{M} \) and equation (5.1).

In particular, \( \mathfrak{M} \) and \( \mathfrak{M}/ \sim \) agree on sentences of \( L \) , and so \( \mathfrak{M}/ \sim \) is the desired model of \( \Gamma \) , in which the equality symbol is interpreted as equality.

### 5.2 Equational and Quantifier-Free Logic

We can get more mileage out of the quotient construction. We will now use it to fulfill a promise made in Section 4.3, namely, to show that equational logic is complete with respect to equational entailments. Fix a language \( L \) without relation symbols and assume that \( L \) contains at least one constant. The following completes the proof of Theorem 4.4.1.

Proposition 5.2.1. Let \( \Gamma \) be a set of universally quantified equations \( \forall \overrightarrow{x}\left( {u = v}\right) \) . If \( s \) and \( t \) are closed terms and \( \Gamma  \vDash  s = t \) , then \( s = t \) is provable from substitution instances of formulas in \( \Gamma \) in the equational fragment of first-order logic.

Proof Let \( L \) be the language of \( \Gamma \) and let \( \mathfrak{M} \) be the term model for \( L \) , that is, the model whose universe is the set of closed terms in \( L \) , interpreting each \( n \) -ary function symbol \( f \) by the function which maps terms \( {r}_{0},\ldots ,{r}_{n - 1} \) to the term \( f\left( {{r}_{0},\ldots ,{r}_{n - 1}}\right) \) . For any two closed terms \( q \) and \( r \) , define \( q \sim  r \) to mean that \( q = r \) is provable from substitution instances of \( \Gamma \) in the equational fragment of first-order logic. It follows directly from the rules for equality that \( \sim \) is an equivalence relation and that it respects the function symbols in the language, in the sense that if \( f \) is an \( n \) -ary function symbol and \( {q}_{i} \sim  {r}_{i} \) for each \( i < n \) , then \( f\left( {{q}_{0},\ldots ,{q}_{n - 1}}\right)  \sim  f\left( {{r}_{0},\ldots ,{r}_{n - 1}}\right) . \)

I claim that \( \mathfrak{M}/ \sim \) is a model of \( \Gamma \) . The construction of \( \mathfrak{M}/ \sim \) was designed to make this true, but let us spell out the details. Suppose \( \forall {x}_{0},\ldots ,{x}_{n - 1}\left( {u\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right)  = v\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) }\right) \) is in \( \Gamma \) and let \( \left\lbrack  {r}_{0}\right\rbrack  ,\ldots ,\left\lbrack  {r}_{n - 1}\right\rbrack \) be any tuple of elements of the universe of \( \mathfrak{M}/ \sim \) . Then under the assignment which maps each \( {x}_{i} \) to \( {r}_{i}, u\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \) denotes \( \left\lbrack  {u\left( \overrightarrow{r}\right) }\right\rbrack \) and \( v\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \) denotes \( \left\lbrack  {v\left( \overrightarrow{r}\right) }\right\rbrack \) . Since the equation \( u\left( \overrightarrow{r}\right)  = v\left( \overrightarrow{r}\right) \) is a substitution instance of a formula in \( \Gamma \) , we have \( u\left( \overrightarrow{r}\right)  \sim  v\left( \overrightarrow{r}\right) \) . This implies \( \left\lbrack  {u\left( \overrightarrow{r}\right) }\right\rbrack   = \left\lbrack  {v\left( \overrightarrow{r}\right) }\right\rbrack \) , as required.

Since \( \Gamma  \vDash  s = t \) , we have that \( s = t \) is true in \( \mathfrak{M}/ \sim \) . But in that model, \( s \) denotes \( \left\lbrack  s\right\rbrack \) and \( t \) denotes \( \left\lbrack  t\right\rbrack \) , so we have \( \left\lbrack  s\right\rbrack   = \left\lbrack  t\right\rbrack \) . This implies \( s \sim  t \) , and, by definition, this means that \( s = t \) is provable from substitution instances of formulas in \( \Gamma \) in the equational fragment of first-order logic.

The restriction to closed terms \( s \) and \( t \) in the previous proposition is only for notational convenience, since, by Proposition 4.2.1, we can replace variables by fresh constants. We can also fulfill another promise from Section 4.4 and show that classical first-order logic is conservative over the deductive system for quantifier-free classical first-order logic.

Proposition 5.2.2. Let \( \Gamma \) be a set of universal sentences and let \( A \) be a quantifier-free formula. If \( \Gamma  \vDash  A \) , then \( A \) is provable from substitution instances of \( \Gamma \) in the quantifier-free fragment of first-order logic.

Proof Once again, replacing variables in \( A \) by fresh constants, it does not hurt to assume that \( A \) is a sentence. Suppose there is no proof of \( A \) from substitution instances of \( \Gamma \) in quantifier-free first-order logic. Let \( \Delta \) be the set of such instances together with the formula \( \neg A \) and all instances of the axioms for equality. Treating every atomic sentence in the language as a propositional formula, we have that \( \Delta \) is propositionally consistent. By the completeness theorem for propositional logic, there is a truth assignment \( v \) that satisfies all the atomic formulas in \( \Delta \) .

Define the relation \( s \sim  t \) on closed formulas to hold if \( v \vDash  s = t \) . Because \( \Delta \) includes all instances of the axioms for equality, we have that \( \sim \) is an equivalence relation and a congruence with respect to all the function and relation symbols. Define a model \( \mathfrak{M} \) whose universe consists of the equivalence classes of closed terms under \( \sim \) by setting \( {f}^{\mathfrak{M}}\left( {\left\lbrack  {t}_{0}\right\rbrack  ,\ldots ,\left\lbrack  {t}_{n - 1}\right\rbrack  }\right)  = \left\lbrack  {f\left( {{t}_{0},\ldots ,{t}_{n - 1}}\right) }\right\rbrack \) as before, and declaring that \( {R}^{\mathfrak{M}}\left( {\left\lbrack  {t}_{0}\right\rbrack  ,\ldots ,\left\lbrack  {t}_{n - 1}\right\rbrack  }\right) \) holds if and only if \( v \vDash  R\left( {{t}_{0},\ldots ,{t}_{n - 1}}\right) \) . By induction on quantifier-free formulas, we have that for every quantifier-free formula \( A\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \) and any tuple of terms \( {t}_{0},\ldots ,{t}_{n - 1} \) , \( \mathfrak{M} \vDash  A\left( {\left\lbrack  {t}_{0}\right\rbrack  ,\ldots ,\left\lbrack  {t}_{n - 1}\right\rbrack  }\right) \) if and only if \( v \vDash  A\left( {{t}_{0},\ldots ,{t}_{n - 1}}\right) \) . In particular, every quantifier-free substitution instance of a formula in \( \Gamma \) is true in \( \mathfrak{M} \) , and hence every sentence in \( \Gamma \) is true in \( \mathfrak{M} \) . But also, since \( \neg A \) is in \( \Delta , A \) is false in \( \mathfrak{M} \) . So there is no first-order proof of \( A \) from \( \Gamma \) .

Exercise 5.2.2 asks you to modify this construction to show that if a quantifier-free formula \( A \) is provable in classical first-order logic, then in fact there is a propositional proof of \( A \) from instances of equality axioms in which all the terms that appear are subterms of terms that occur in \( A \) . Since there are only finitely many such axioms, this provides a decision procedure for the quantifier-free fragment of first-order logic, and hence the universal fragment as well. (See Theorem 4.4.4.)

### 5.3 Intuitionistic Logic

In Chapter 3, we saw that Kripke models provide a natural generalization of classical truth-table semantics to intuitionistic and minimal logic. In this section, we will see that classical model-theoretic semantics carries over in a similar way. Once again, we will start with first-order logic without equality. A Kripke structure \( \mathfrak{K} \) for a language \( L \) consists of a partial order \( \left( {K, \leq  }\right) \) together with an assignment of a structure, \( {\mathfrak{K}}_{\alpha } \) , to each node \( \alpha  \in  K \) , in such a way that the following conditions hold for every \( \alpha \) and every \( \beta  \leq  \alpha \) :

- \( \left| {\mathfrak{K}}_{\beta }\right|  \supseteq  \left| {\mathfrak{K}}_{\alpha }\right| \) .

- If \( \overrightarrow{a} \in  \left| {\mathfrak{K}}_{\alpha }\right| ,{f}^{{\mathfrak{K}}_{\alpha }}\left( \overrightarrow{a}\right)  = {f}^{{\mathfrak{K}}_{\beta }}\left( \overrightarrow{a}\right) \) .

- If \( \overrightarrow{a} \in  \left| {\mathfrak{K}}_{\alpha }\right| \) and \( {R}^{{\mathfrak{K}}_{\alpha }}\left( \overrightarrow{a}\right) \) , then \( {R}^{{\mathfrak{K}}_{\beta }}\left( \overrightarrow{a}\right) \) .

As with Kripke semantics for propositional logic, think of \( \beta  \leq  \alpha \) as asserting that \( \beta \) is stronger than \( \alpha \) , in the sense that it provides more information or corresponds to a later point in time. The monotonicity clauses then assert that when we pass to a stronger condition, more elements of the domain become visible, and more atomic facts are seen to be true.

The second clause guarantees that the interpretation of the function symbols on old elements remains stable as new elements appear.

Let \( \mathfrak{K} \) be a Kripke structure for \( L \) . As for classical logic, given any term of \( L \) and any assignment \( \sigma \) of elements of \( \left| {\mathfrak{K}}_{\alpha }\right| \) to variables, each term \( t \) of \( L \) has a denotation \( \llbracket t{\rrbracket }^{\alpha ,\sigma } \) . The forcing relation \( \alpha { \Vdash  }_{\sigma }A \) is now defined by recursion on formulas \( A \) , where in each case \( \sigma \) is assumed to be an assignment of variables to elements of \( \left| {\mathfrak{K}}_{\alpha }\right| \) . In the base case, where \( A \) is atomic, we use the classical notion of truth in \( {\mathfrak{K}}_{\alpha } \) , and the clauses for the propositional connectives carry over from Section 3.3. Only the clauses for the quantifiers are new.

- \( \alpha { \Vdash  }_{\sigma }A \) if and only if \( {\mathfrak{K}}_{\alpha }{ \vDash  }_{\sigma }A \) , when \( A \) is an atomic formula.

- \( \alpha { \Vdash  }_{\sigma }A \land  B \) if and only if \( \alpha  \Vdash  A \) and \( \alpha  \Vdash  B \) .

- \( \alpha { \Vdash  }_{\sigma }A \vee  B \) if and only if \( \alpha  \Vdash  A \) or \( \alpha  \Vdash  B \) .

- \( \alpha { \Vdash  }_{\sigma }A \rightarrow  B \) if and only if \( \forall \beta  \leq  \alpha \left( {\beta  \Vdash  A \rightarrow  \beta  \Vdash  B}\right) \) .

- \( \alpha { \Vdash  }_{\sigma }\exists {xA}\left( x\right) \) if and only if \( \exists a \in  \left| {\mathfrak{K}}_{\alpha }\right| \left( {\alpha { \Vdash  }_{\sigma \left\lbrack  {x \mapsto  a}\right\rbrack  }A}\right) \)

- \( \alpha { \Vdash  }_{\sigma }\forall {xA}\left( x\right) \) if and only if \( \forall \beta  \leq  \alpha \forall a \in  \left| {\mathfrak{K}}_{\beta }\right| \left( {\beta { \Vdash  }_{\sigma \left\lbrack  {x \mapsto  a}\right\rbrack  }A}\right) \)

In words, \( \alpha \) forces \( \exists {xA}\left( x\right) \) if there is an element \( a \) in the universe at \( \alpha \) such that \( \alpha \) forces \( A\left( a\right) \) . The clause for \( \forall {xA}\left( x\right) \) is more interesting: to say that \( \alpha \) forces \( \forall {xA}\left( x\right) \) , we need to know not only that \( A \) is forced to hold of every element of the current domain, but, moreover, if any elements appear at a later node, \( \beta , A \) is forced to hold of those elements too. As with propositional logic, to extend the interpretation from minimal to intuitionistic logic, we add:

- \( \alpha { \nVdash  }_{\sigma } \bot \)

Once again, a straightforward induction on formulas yields monotonicity:

Lemma 5.3.1. For every \( \alpha ,\beta \) , and \( \sigma \) , if \( \alpha { \Vdash  }_{\sigma }A \) and \( \beta  \leq  \alpha \) , then \( \beta { \Vdash  }_{\sigma }A \) .

Proof The most interesting case involves the universal quantifier. Indeed, in this case the definition of forcing is designed just for this purpose. Suppose \( \alpha { \Vdash  }_{\sigma }\forall {xA} \) and \( \beta  \leq  \alpha \) . By the clause for forcing for the universal quantifier, we know that for every \( \gamma  \leq  \alpha \) and every \( a \in  \left| {\mathfrak{K}}_{\alpha }\right| ,\gamma { \Vdash  }_{\sigma \left\lbrack  {x \mapsto  a}\right\rbrack  }A \) . Since any \( \gamma \) less than or equal to \( \beta \) is also less than or equal to \( \alpha \) , the previous statement holds with \( \beta \) in place of \( \alpha \) .

The case of the existential quantifier is handled by the fact that for every \( \beta  \leq  \alpha \) , we have \( \left| {\mathfrak{K}}_{\beta }\right|  \supseteq  \left| {\mathfrak{K}}_{\alpha }\right| \)

It is not hard to verify that the analogue of Proposition 5.1.2, the substitution lemma, holds for Kripke models as well. In other words, if \( a = \llbracket t{\rrbracket }^{{\mathfrak{K}}_{\alpha },\sigma } \) , then \( \alpha { \Vdash  }_{\sigma }A\left\lbrack  {t/x}\right\rbrack \) if and only if \( \alpha { \Vdash  }_{\sigma \left\lbrack  {x \mapsto  a}\right\rbrack  }A \) . In particular, if \( x \) does not occur in \( A \) and \( \alpha { \Vdash  }_{\sigma }A \) , then \( \alpha { \Vdash  }_{\sigma }A \) , then \( \alpha { \Vdash  }_{\sigma \left\lbrack  {x \mapsto  a}\right\rbrack  }A \) for every \( a \) .

We now establish the soundness of first-order minimal and intuitionistic logic with respect the Kripke semantics, still restricting our attention to first-order logic without equality. If \( \mathfrak{K} \) is a Kripke model, \( \alpha \) is any node, \( \sigma \) is any assignment of elements of \( \left| {\mathfrak{K}}_{\alpha }\right| \) to variables, and \( \Gamma \) is a set of sentences, say \( \alpha { \Vdash  }_{\sigma }\Gamma \) if and only if \( \alpha { \Vdash  }_{\sigma }B \) for every \( B \) in \( \Gamma \) . If \( \Gamma  \cup  \{ A\} \) is a set of sentences, say \( \Gamma  \Vdash  A \) if, for every Kripke model \( \mathfrak{K} \) and node \( \alpha \) such that \( \alpha  \Vdash  \Gamma \) , we have \( \alpha  \Vdash  A \) .

Theorem 5.3.2. For every set of sentences \( \Gamma \) and every sentence \( A \) , if \( \Gamma  \vdash  A \) , then \( \Gamma  \Vdash  A \) .

Proof We fix a Kripke model \( \mathfrak{K} \) and show the following by induction on derivations: for every derivable sequent \( \Gamma  \vdash  A \) , every node \( \alpha \) , and every assignment \( \sigma \) , if \( \alpha { \Vdash  }_{\sigma }\Gamma \) , then \( \alpha { \Vdash  }_{\sigma }A \) . The proof is similar to the proof of soundness for propositional logic, Theorem 3.3.7. To handle the introduction rule for the universal quantifier, suppose the last line of the derivation obtains \( \Gamma  \vdash  \forall {xA} \) from \( \Gamma  \vdash  A \) , and suppose \( \alpha { \Vdash  }_{\sigma }\Gamma \) . We need to show \( \alpha { \Vdash  }_{\sigma }\forall {xA} \) , so suppose \( \beta  \leq  \alpha \) and \( a \in  \left| {\mathfrak{K}}_{\beta }\right| \) . By monotonicity and the fact that \( x \) does not occur in \( \Gamma \) , we have \( \beta { \Vdash  }_{\sigma \left\lbrack  {x \mapsto  a}\right\rbrack  }\Gamma \) . By the inductive hypothesis, this yields \( \beta { \Vdash  }_{\sigma \left\lbrack  {x \mapsto  a}\right\rbrack  }A \) , as required.

For the \( \forall \) elimination rule, suppose the last line of the derivation obtains \( \Gamma  \vdash  A\left\lbrack  {t/x}\right\rbrack \) from \( \Gamma  \vdash  \forall {xA} \) and suppose \( \alpha { \Vdash  }_{\sigma }\Gamma \) . By the inductive hypothesis, we have \( \alpha { \Vdash  }_{\sigma }\forall {xA} \) . Let \( a = \; \llbracket t{\rrbracket }^{{\mathfrak{K}}_{\alpha },\sigma } \) . Since \( \alpha  \leq  \alpha \) , we have \( \alpha { \Vdash  }_{\sigma \left\lbrack  {x \mapsto  a}\right\rbrack  }A \) , and hence \( \alpha { \Vdash  }_{\sigma }A\left\lbrack  {t/x}\right\rbrack \) .

The rules for the existential quantifier are left to you.

The proof of the completeness theorem for intuitionistic and minimal first-order logic without equality is similar to the proof of completeness for intuitionistic and minimal propositional logic. Given a set of sentences \( \Gamma \) and a sentence \( A \) , if \( \Gamma \) does not prove \( A \) , we want to build a Kripke model with a node that forces \( \Gamma \) but not \( A \) . Section 5.1 used classical logic to extend \( \Gamma \) to a Henkin set, enabling us to build a syntactic model. We do not have that option here, so we need use another strategy to add constants to the language. To that end, we generalize the notion of a saturated set from Definition 3.3.5.

Definition 5.3.3. A set of first-order sentences \( \Gamma \) is saturated if the following hold:

- \( \Gamma \) is deductively closed.

- If \( A \vee  B \in  \Gamma \) , then \( A \in  \Gamma \) or \( B \in  \Gamma \) .

- If \( \exists {xA}\left( x\right)  \in  \Gamma \) , then \( A\left( c\right)  \in  \Gamma \) for some \( c \) .

When dealing with intuitionistic logic instead of minimal logic, we also require that \( \Gamma \) is consistent.

In analogy to Lemma 3.3.6 for propositional logic, we have:

Lemma 5.3.4. Let \( \Gamma \) be a set of sentences in a language \( L \) and \( A \) be a sentence in \( L \) , and suppose \( \Gamma  \nvdash  A \) . Then there is a saturated set \( {\Gamma }^{\prime } \supseteq  \Gamma \) in a larger language, \( {L}^{\prime } \) , such that \( {\Gamma }^{\prime } \nvdash  A \) .

Proof As usual, we will focus on the case where \( L \) is countable. Let \( {L}^{\prime } \) be a language with countably many new constants \( {c}_{0},{c}_{1},{c}_{2},\ldots \) . The proof is similar to the proof of Lemma 3.3.6 for the propositional case, except we now divide the construction into even and odd steps. Start with \( {\Gamma }_{0} \) equal to the deductive closure of \( \Gamma \) . In step \( {2i} \) , we consider a formula \( {C}_{i} \vee  {D}_{i} \) in \( {\Gamma }_{2i} \) , and add one or the other to \( {\Gamma }_{{2i} + 1} \) , as in the proof of Lemma 3.3.6. At stage \( {2i} + 1 \) we take the \( i \) th formula \( \exists x{C}_{i}\left( x\right) \) in an enumeration of \( {L}^{\prime } \) . If it is in \( {\Gamma }_{{2i} + 1} \) , we set \( {\Gamma }_{{2i} + 2} \) to be the set of all deductive consequences of \( {\Gamma }_{{2i} + 1} \) together with \( {C}_{i}\left( c\right) \) , for a constant \( c \) that has not been used yet. Finally, we set \( {\Gamma }^{\prime } = \mathop{\bigcup }\limits_{i}{\Gamma }_{i} \) .

Theorem 5.3.5. For every set of sentences \( \Gamma \) and every sentence \( A \) , if \( \Gamma  \Vdash  A \) then \( \Gamma  \vdash  A \) .

As in Section 3.3, we will construct a single Kripke model \( \mathfrak{K} \) that is rich enough to serve as a counterexample for any entailment in \( L \) that is not provable. First, construct a sequence of languages \( L = {L}_{0},{L}_{1},{L}_{2},\ldots \) where each augments the previous one with countably many new constants. A node of \( \mathfrak{K} \) is any consistent, saturated set of sentences in \( {L}_{i} \) , for some \( i \) . If \( \alpha \) and \( \beta \) are nodes of \( \mathfrak{K} \) , then \( \beta  \leq  \alpha \) if and only if \( \beta  \supseteq  \alpha \) .

If \( \alpha \) is a saturated subset of \( {L}_{i} \) , the universe of \( {\mathfrak{K}}_{\alpha } \) consists of the closed terms of \( {L}_{i} \) . We interpret the function and relation symbols of \( L \) as in Section 5.1 so that for any atomic sentence \( A \) of \( {L}_{i},{\mathfrak{K}}_{\alpha } \vDash  A \) if and only if \( A \in  \Gamma \) . The fact that each node \( \alpha \) is contained in one of the languages \( {L}_{i} \) enables us to use Lemma 5.3.4 to find extensions of \( \alpha \) that are saturated in \( {L}_{i + 1} \) , which has countably many new constants. The completeness theorem for first-order logic without equality is therefore a consequence of the following.

Theorem 5.3.6. In the Kripke model \( \mathfrak{K} \) just described, given any node \( \alpha \) in the language \( {L}_{i} \) , any formula \( A\left( {{x}_{0},\ldots ,{x}_{n - 1}}\right) \) in \( {L}_{i} \) with the free variables shown, and any assignment \( \sigma \) of variables to closed terms, \( \alpha { \Vdash  }_{\sigma }A \) if and only if \( A\left( {\sigma \left( {x}_{0}\right) ,\ldots ,\sigma \left( {x}_{n - 1}\right) }\right)  \in  \alpha \) .

Proof The proof is by induction on \( A \) . In the base case where \( A \) is atomic, the claim follows immediately from the construction. The propositional connectives are handled just as in the proof of completeness for propositional logic, Theorem 3.3.7, so we only have to consider the cases for the quantifiers.

Suppose \( \forall {yA}\left( {y,\sigma \left( {x}_{0}\right) ,\ldots ,\sigma \left( {x}_{n - 1}\right) }\right)  \in  \alpha \) , and suppose \( \beta  \leq  \alpha \) . We need to show that for every element \( a \in  \left| {M}_{\beta }\right| ,\beta { \Vdash  }_{\sigma \left\lbrack  {y \mapsto  a}\right\rbrack  }A\left( {y,{x}_{0},\ldots ,{x}_{n - 1}}\right) \) . By the inductive hypothesis, this happens if and only if \( A\left( {a,\sigma \left( {x}_{0}\right) ,\ldots ,\sigma \left( {x}_{n - 1}\right) }\right)  \in  \beta \) , and the latter follows from \( \beta  \supseteq  \alpha \) and the fact that \( \alpha \) is closed under provability.

Conversely, suppose \( \forall {yA}\left( {y,\sigma \left( {x}_{0}\right) ,\ldots ,\sigma \left( {x}_{n - 1}\right) }\right)  \notin  \alpha \) . Then, choosing a constant \( c \) in \( {L}_{i + 1} \) , we have \( \alpha  \nvdash  A\left( {c,\sigma \left( {x}_{0}\right) ,\ldots ,\sigma \left( {x}_{n - 1}\right) }\right) \) . By Lemma 5.3.4, there is a saturated set \( \beta \) in \( {L}_{i + 2} \) such that \( A\left( {c,\sigma \left( {x}_{0}\right) ,\ldots ,\sigma \left( {x}_{n - 1}\right) }\right)  \notin  \beta \) . By the inductive hypothesis, we have \( \beta {\parallel }_{{f}_{\sigma \left\lbrack  {y \mapsto  c}\right\rbrack  }} \; A\left( {\sigma \left( y\right) ,\sigma \left( {x}_{0}\right) ,\ldots ,\sigma \left( {x}_{n - 1}\right) }\right) \) . Since \( \beta  \leq  \alpha \) , this means \( \alpha { \nVdash  }_{\sigma }\forall {yA}\left( {y,\sigma \left( {x}_{0}\right) ,\ldots ,\sigma \left( {x}_{n - 1}\right) }\right) \) .

The case for the existential quantifier is straightforward, given that a sentence \( \exists {yA}\left( y\right) \) is in a saturated set \( \alpha \) if and only if \( A\left( c\right) \) is in \( \alpha \) for some constant \( c \) .

We now define a Kripke model for first-order logic with equality to be a Kripke model equipped with an equivalence relation at each node that respects the interpretations of the functions and relations and is preserved when we pass to a stronger node. Equivalently, a Kripke model for first-order logic with equality is just a Kripke model in which equality is interpreted as a relation satisfying the equality axioms. In that case, there is nothing more to say: a formula \( A \) is provable from \( \Gamma \) in first-order logic with equality just in case it is provable from \( \Gamma \) and the equality axioms in first-order logic without special treatment of equality, and the completeness of natural deduction with respect to the former the former follows its completeness with respect to the latter.

In general, we cannot apply a quotient construction to obtain a Kripke model where the equality symbol is interpreted by equality at every node. For example, to show that \( \forall x, y(x = \; y \vee  x \neq  y \) ) is not intuitionistically valid, we need a Kripke model and a node with elements \( a, b \) such that \( a \) and \( b \) are not equal at that node but become equal later on. Our definition of a Kripke model does not accommodate collapses of this sort. However, Exercise 5.3.1 asks you to show that for intuitionistic logic with the additional axiom \( \forall x, y\left( {x = y \vee  x \neq  y}\right) \) , we can restrict attention to Kripke models in which the equality symbol is interpreted by equality. Such models are called normal.

As for propositional logic, we can use Kripke models to show unprovability.

The variations on propositional Kripke models discussed at the end of Section 3.3 carry over to first-order logic. ### 5.4 Algebraic Semantics

Definition 3.4.2 tells us that a Boolean algebra or Heyting algebra is complete if it is closed under arbitrary meets and joins. We can generalize classical model-theoretic semantics so that formulas take values in an arbitrary complete Boolean algebra \( \mathfrak{B} \) using these meets and joins to interpret the quantifiers. Such a model \( \mathfrak{M} \) consists of a nonempty set, \( \left| \mathfrak{M}\right| \) , an interpretation of each \( n \) -ary function symbol \( f \) as an \( n \) -ary function \( {f}^{\mathfrak{M}} \) on \( \left| \mathfrak{M}\right| \) , and an interpretation of each \( n \) -ary relation symbol \( R \) as an \( n \) -ary function from \( {\left| \mathfrak{M}\right| }^{n} \rightarrow  \mathfrak{B} \) , providing \( {R}^{\mathfrak{M}}\left( {{a}_{0},\ldots ,{a}_{n - 1}}\right) \) with a truth value in \( \mathfrak{B} \) for each \( n \) -tuple \( \overrightarrow{a} \) in \( \left| \mathfrak{M}\right| \) . The resulting structure is known as a Boolean-valued model. As before, every term \( t \) evaluates to an element \( {t}^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma } \) in \( \left| \mathfrak{M}\right| \) given an assignment \( \sigma \) to the free variables. We can then evaluate atomic formulas \( \llbracket R\left( {{t}_{0},\ldots ,{t}_{n - 1}}\right) {\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma } = {R}^{\mathfrak{M}}\left( {{t}_{0}^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma },\ldots ,{t}_{n - 1}^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma }}\right) \) and extend the means of evaluating the propositional connectives described in Section 3.2 with the following clauses for the quantifiers:

- \( \llbracket \forall {xA}\left( x\right) {\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma } = \mathop{\bigwedge }\limits_{{a \in  \left| \mathfrak{M}\right| }}\llbracket A{\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma \left\lbrack  {x \mapsto  a}\right\rbrack  } \)

- \( \llbracket \exists {xA}\left( x\right) {\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma } = \mathop{\bigvee }\limits_{{a \in  \left| \mathfrak{M}\right| }}\llbracket A{\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma \left\lbrack  {x \mapsto  a}\right\rbrack  } \) .

The usual model-theoretic semantics is the special case where \( \mathfrak{B} \) is the two-element complete Boolean algebra \( \{ \top , \bot  \} \) .

It is straightforward to establish soundness. As in the proof of soundness for propositional logic with respect to Boolean-valued semantics, we want to show that if a sequent \( \Gamma  \vdash  A \) is provable, then, for any \( \sigma ,\mathop{\bigwedge }\limits_{{B \in  \Gamma }}{\left\lbrack  B\right\rbrack  }^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma } \leq  {\left\lbrack  A\right\rbrack  }^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma } \) . For convenience, I will abbreviate the left-hand side of that inequality as \( \llbracket \Gamma {\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma } \) . Let us consider the natural deduction rules for the existential quantifier. For the introduction rule, suppose for some \( t \) we have \( \llbracket \Gamma {\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma } \leq \; \llbracket A\left( t\right) {\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma } \) . Since

\[

we have \( \llbracket \Gamma {\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma } \leq  \llbracket A\left( t\right) {\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma } \) , as required. For the elimination rule, suppose we have \( \llbracket \Gamma {\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma } \leq  \llbracket \exists {xA}\left( x\right) {\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma } \) and \( \llbracket \Gamma , A\left( x\right) {\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma } \leq  \llbracket B{\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma } \) for every \( \sigma \) , where \( x \) is not free in \( \Gamma \) or \( B \) . Theorem 5.4.1. Classical logic is sound and complete with respect to Boolean-valued models.

What happens if, instead, we evaluate formulas with respect to a complete Heyting algebra instead of a complete Boolean algebra? Establishing the soundness of intuitionistic logic with respect to this semantics is straightforward. Let us show that intuitionistic logic is also complete with respect to this semantics. We will focus on first-order logic without equality, since we can handle equality by interpreting it as a relation symbol satisfying the equality axioms, as we did in the last section.

The construction we will use is similar to the proof of completeness for classical propositional logic via the Tarski-Lindenbaum algebra that was presented in Section 3.2. Let \( \Gamma \) be a set of sentences in some language, \( L \) . We want to show that for any formula \( A \) , if \( \Gamma  \vDash  A \) , then \( \Gamma  \vdash  A \) . We will take the universe of \( \mathfrak{M} \) to consist of all terms in \( L \) , including terms with free variables. Let \( S \) be the set of all formulas in \( L \) , including formulas with free variables, and say two such formulas \( A \) and \( B \) are equivalent, written \( A \equiv  B \) , if \( \Gamma  \vdash  A \leftrightarrow  B \) in intuitionistic logic. Let \( \mathfrak{H} = S/ \equiv \) .

It is straightforward to show that \( \mathfrak{H} \) has the structure of a Heyting algebra when we define \( A \leq  B \) by \( \Gamma  \vdash  A \rightarrow  B \) . The meets, joins, and pseudocomplements are induced on the quotient by the syntactic operations of forming conjunctions, disjunctions, and implications.

\( \mathfrak{H} \) is not a complete algebra; we will deal with that in a moment. But, in \( \mathfrak{H} \) , we have that \( \left\lbrack  {\exists {xA}\left( x\right) }\right\rbrack \) is the least upper bound for the elements \( \left\lbrack  {\exists {xA}\left( t\right) }\right\rbrack \) as \( t \) ranges over terms in the language, and \( \forall {xA}\left( x\right) \) is the greatest lower bound for those elements. For the first claim, it is straightforward that \( \left\lbrack  {A\left( t\right) }\right\rbrack   \leq  \left\lbrack  {\exists {xA}\left( x\right) }\right\rbrack \) for every term \( t \) , so \( \left\lbrack  {\exists {xA}\left( x\right) }\right\rbrack \) is an upper bound on the set of elements \( \left\lbrack  {A\left( t\right) }\right\rbrack \) . To show that it is the least such, suppose that \( B \) is another upper bound, so that we have \( \left\lbrack  {A\left( t\right) }\right\rbrack   \leq  \left\lbrack  B\right\rbrack \) for every term \( t \) . This means that \( \Gamma \) proves \( A\left( t\right)  \rightarrow  B \) for every term \( t \) . Taking \( t \) to be a variable \( x \) that does not occur in \( B \) , we have that \( \Gamma  \vdash  \exists {xA}\left( x\right)  \rightarrow  B \) , and so \( \left\lbrack  {\exists {xA}\left( x\right) }\right\rbrack   \leq  \left\lbrack  B\right\rbrack \) , as required.

As was the case with the syntactic construction of a two-valued model in Section 5.1, we have \( \llbracket t\left( {{x}_{0},\ldots ,{x}_{n - 1}}\right) {\rrbracket }^{\mathfrak{M},\sigma } = t\left( {\sigma \left( {x}_{0}\right) ,\ldots ,\sigma \left( {x}_{n - 1}\right) }\right) \) for every term \( t \) . Our model takes values in \( \mathfrak{H} \) , which we only know to be a Heyting algebra, rather than a complete Heyting algebra. We have established that \( \mathfrak{H} \) has enough meets and joins to interpret the quantifiers. The good news is that lattice theory provides various ways of embedding lattices into larger ones with better closure properties. This is the result we need:

Theorem 5.4.2. Every Heyting algebra can be embedded in a complete Heyting algebra in a way that preserves existing meets and joins.

The construction we will use to prove Theorem 5.4.2 is known as the MacNeille completion, and it will take us on a detour through some lattice theory.

Let \( \left( {P, \leq  }\right) \) be any partial order. An order ideal is any subset \( S \) of \( P \) that is closed downward, that is, such that \( x \in  S \) and \( y \leq  x \) imply \( y \in  S \) for any \( x, y \in  P \) . An order filter is, dually, a subset of \( S \) that is closed upward. Given \( x \in  P \) , the set \( \downarrow  \left( x\right)  = \{ y \mid  y \leq  x\} \) is clearly an order ideal, and the set \( \uparrow  \left( x\right)  = \{ y \mid  y \leq  x\} \) is clearly an order filter. These are called the principal order ideal and principal order filter generated by \( x \) , respectively.

Given a subset \( S \subseteq  P \) , let \( u\left( S\right) \) denote the set of all upper bounds on \( S \) , that is, the set of element \( y \) such that \( y \geq  x \) for every \( x \) in \( S \) . Dually, let \( l\left( S\right) \) be the set of lower bounds on \( S \) . Define a cut in \( P \) to be a pair \( \left( {L, U}\right) \) of subsets of \( P \) such that \( L = l\left( U\right) \) and \( U = u\left( L\right) \) . It is easy to check that if \( \left( {{L}_{0},{U}_{0}}\right) \) and \( \left( {{L}_{1},{U}_{1}}\right) \) are cuts, then \( {L}_{0} \subseteq  {L}_{1} \) if and only if \( {U}_{0} \subseteq  {U}_{1} \) . When this happens, we say \( \left( {{L}_{0},{U}_{0}}\right)  \leq  \left( {{L}_{1},{U}_{1}}\right) \) . Clearly this defines a partial order on the set of all cuts.

The MacNeille completion of \( \left( {P, \leq  }\right) \) is defined to be the set of all cuts in \( P \) with the order just defined. If \( x \) is any element of \( P \) , then \( \left( { \downarrow  \left( x\right) , \uparrow  \left( x\right) }\right) \) is a cut. We define a function \( \varphi \) from \( \left( {P, \leq  }\right) \) to its MacNeille completion by \( \varphi \left( x\right)  = \left( { \downarrow  \left( x\right) , \uparrow  \left( x\right) }\right) \) . For every \( x \) and \( y \) in \( P \) have \( x \leq  y \) if and only if \( \varphi \left( x\right)  \leq  \varphi \left( y\right) \) , so \( \varphi \) is an order-preserving embedding. (An exercise below asks you to check that for any subset \( S \) of a partial order, \( S \subseteq  u\left( {l\left( S\right) }\right) \) . The conditions \( L = l\left( U\right) \) and \( U = u\left( L\right) \) on a cut imply \( L = l\left( {u\left( L\right) }\right) \) , and given any set \( L \) satisfying this condition, we can recover \( U \) as \( U = u\left( L\right) \) . Thus we can equivalently take the MacNeille completion to be the collection of sets \( L \) satisfying \( L = l\left( {u\left( L\right) }\right) \) , ordered by inclusion.)

In Section 3.2, we came across the notion of a filter in a bounded lattice. The dual notion is that of an ideal: a subset \( I \) of a lattice \( \left( {L, \leq  }\right) \) is an ideal if it is an order ideal such that \( \bot   \in  I \) and \( x \vee  y \) is in \( I \) whenever \( x \) and \( y \) are in \( I \) . I leave it as an exercise to check that for any subset \( S \) of a bounded lattice, \( u\left( S\right) \) is always a filter and \( l\left( S\right) \) is always an ideal. The next two propositions tell us that the MacNeille completion of a Heyting algebra satisfies the conclusion of Theorem 5.4.2.

Proposition 5.4.3. For any partial order \( \left( {P, \leq  }\right) \) , the MacNeille completion of \( P \) is a bounded complete lattice, and the embedding \( \varphi \) preserves all meets and joins that exist in \( P \) .

Proof Clearly \( \left( {\varnothing , P}\right) \) is the least element of the MacNeille completion and \( \left( {P,\varnothing }\right) \) is the greatest element. \[

\]

The dual argument shows preservation of joins.

Remember from Section 3.4 that in any Heyting algebra, the relative pseudocomplement \( x \rightarrow  y \) can be characterized as \( \bigvee \{ z \mid  x \land  z \leq  y\} \) . So if \( \left( {P, \leq  }\right) \) is a Heyting algebra in Proposition 5.4.3, that part of the structure is preserved by the embedding. But that doesn't guarantee that the MacNeille completion itself is a Heyting algebra. By Exercise 3.4.3, we would have that conclusion if we could show that the MacNeille completion is a distributive lattice, but, unfortunately, this is not always the case. Instead, we prove the result directly.

Proposition 5.4.4. If \( \mathfrak{H} \) is a Heyting algebra, then its MacNeille completion is a Heyting algebra as well.

Proof Given cuts \( \left( {L, U}\right) \) and \( \left( {{L}^{\prime },{U}^{\prime }}\right) \) , define

\[
\widehat{L} = \left\{  {x \in  H\mid \forall y \in  L\left( {x \land  y \in  {L}^{\prime }}\right) }\right\}  ,
\]

and let \( \widehat{U} = u\left( \widehat{L}\right) \) . I claim

- \( \left( {\widehat{L},\widehat{U}}\right) \) is a cut, that is, \( \widehat{L} = l\left( \widehat{U}\right)  = l\left( {u\left( \widehat{L}\right) }\right) \) , and

- \( \left( {\widehat{L},\widehat{U}}\right) \) is the relative pseudocomplement of \( \left( {L, U}\right) \) and \( \left( {{L}^{\prime },{U}^{\prime }}\right) \) .

To prove the first claim, since the forward inclusion always holds, let \( x \) be an element of \( l\left( {u\left( \widehat{L}\right) }\right) \) and let us show that \( x \) is in \( \widehat{L} \) . To that end, let \( y \) be any element of \( L \) ; it suffices to show \( x \land  y \) is in \( {L}^{\prime } \) . Since \( {L}^{\prime } = l\left( {U}^{\prime }\right) \) , it suffices to show \( x \land  y \leq  z \) for any \( z \) in \( {U}^{\prime } \) . So let \( z \) be any element of \( {U}^{\prime } \) . Since \( \mathfrak{H} \) is a Heyting algebra, \( x \land  y \leq  z \) is equivalent to \( x \leq  y \rightarrow  z \) . Since \( x \) is in \( l\left( {u\left( \widehat{L}\right) }\right) \) , it suffices to show \( y \rightarrow  z \) is in \( u\left( \widehat{L}\right) \) . Let \( w \) be any element of \( \widehat{L} \) ; we need to show \( w \leq  y \rightarrow  z \) . Since \( y \) is in \( L \) , the definition of \( \widehat{L} \) gives \( w \land  y \in  {L}^{\prime } \) , and since \( z \) is in \( {U}^{\prime } \) , we have \( w \land  y \leq  z \) . Since \( \mathfrak{H} \) is a Heyting algebra, this is equivalent to \( w \leq  y \rightarrow  z \) , as required.

To prove the second claim, in order to show that \( \left( {\widehat{L},\widehat{U}}\right) \) is the relative pseudocomplement \( \left( {L, U}\right)  \rightarrow  \left( {{L}^{\prime },{U}^{\prime }}\right) \) , we need to show that for any cut \( \left( {\bar{L},\bar{U}}\right) \) , we have \( \left( {\bar{L},\bar{U}}\right)  \leq  \left( {\widehat{L},\widehat{U}}\right) \) if and only if \( \left( {\bar{L},\bar{U}}\right)  \land  \left( {L, U}\right)  \leq  \left( {{L}^{\prime },{U}^{\prime }}\right) \) . By the definition of wedge and the order in the MacNeille completion, this is equivalent to showing that for any cut \( \left( {\bar{L},\bar{U}}\right) ,\bar{L} \cap  L \subseteq  {L}^{\prime } \) if and only if \( \bar{L} \subseteq  \widehat{L} \) .

Suppose the left-hand side holds and \( x \in  \bar{L} \) . To show \( x \in  \widehat{L} \) , suppose \( y \in  L \) . Since \( \bar{L} \) and \( L \) are both closed downward, \( x \land  y \) is in \( \bar{L} \cap  L \) , and hence in \( {L}^{\prime } \) , as required. Conversely, suppose the right-hand side holds and \( x \) is in \( L \cap  L \) . Then \( x \) is in \( \widehat{L} \) , and so \( x \land  x = x \in  {L}^{\prime } \) , as required.

Thus, we have shown:

Theorem 5.4.5. Intuitionistic logic is sound and complete with respect to Heyting-valued semantics.

### 5.5 Definability

In this section and the next, we consider some results of classical model theory. Even though the focus of this book is on provability rather than semantics, here we will see that semantic notions provide a helpful perspective on the meaning of a formal system. Of course, they are also often interesting in their own right.

Given a formal language and a notion of satisfaction \( \mathfrak{M} \vDash  A\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \) , we are often interested in the extent to which expressions in the language can be used to describe relations and structures of interest. In this section we will consider two notions of definability:

- A set of sentences \( \Gamma \) is said to define the class of structures \( \{ \mathfrak{M} \mid  \mathfrak{M} \vDash  \Gamma \} \) . Similarly, a single sentence \( A \) is said to define the class \( \{ \mathfrak{M} \mid  \mathfrak{M} \vDash  A\} \) . (Since any set can serve as the universe of a structure, the class of structures defined by a set of sentences or a single sentence is generally too large to be a set.)

- Fixing a model \( \mathfrak{M} \) , a formula \( A\left( {{x}_{0},\ldots ,{x}_{n - 1}}\right) \) is said to define the \( n \) -ary relation \( R \) on \( \left| \mathfrak{M}\right| \) with the property that, for any tuple \( \overrightarrow{a}, R\left( \overrightarrow{a}\right) \) holds if and only if \( \mathfrak{M} \vDash  A\left( \overrightarrow{a}\right) \) . Similarly, a formula \( A\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) \) is said to define the function \( f\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \) on \( \mathfrak{M} \) if it defines the graph of \( f \) , that is, the relation \( f\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right)  = y \) . More precisely, if \( A\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \) is a formula in the language of the model, \( A\left( \overrightarrow{x}\right) \) is said to define the corresponding relation without parameters. Recall from Section 5.1 that, given a tuple of parameters \( \overrightarrow{b} \) from the universe of \( \mathfrak{M} \) , we can talk about a formula \( A\left( {\overrightarrow{x},\overrightarrow{b}}\right) \) that uses those parameters. A function or relation that is defined by such a formula is said to be definable from the parameters \( \overrightarrow{b} \) .

Here are some examples of definability in the first sense:

- A partial order is a structure with a binary relation satisfying the following:

\[
- \forall x\left( {x \leq  x}\right)
\]

\[
- \forall x, y, z\left( {x \leq  y \land  y \leq  z \rightarrow  x \leq  z}\right)
\]

\[
- \forall x, y\left( {x \leq  y \land  y \leq  x \rightarrow  x = y}\right) .
\]

In other words, the set with these three axioms defines the class of partial orders. Adding the axiom \( \forall x, y\left( {x \leq  y \vee  y \leq  x}\right) \) yields the class of total, or linear orders. If we define \( x < y \) to mean \( x \leq  y \land  x \neq  y \) , adding the axiom \( \forall x, y\left( {x < y \rightarrow  \exists z\left( {x < z \land  z < y}\right) }\right) \) to that yields the class of dense linear orders.

- In the first-order language with no function or relation symbols other than equality, a structure is a set. For every \( n \) , the sentence

\[
{A}_{n} \equiv  \exists {x}_{0},\ldots ,{x}_{n - 1}\mathop{\bigwedge }\limits_{{i \neq  j}}{x}_{i} \neq  {x}_{j}
\]

defines the class of structures with at least \( n \) distinct elements. The set of sentences \( {\left\{  {A}_{n}\right\}  }_{n \in  \mathbb{N}} \) defines the class of infinite structures.

- A directed graph is just a structure with a binary relation \( E \) . Here \( E\left( {a, b}\right) \) is interpreted as the statement that there is an edge from \( a \) to \( b \) . It is sometimes useful to rule out self-loops by adding the axiom \( \exists x\neg E\left( {x, x}\right) \) . Edges in a directed graph have a direction, so \( E\left( {a, b}\right) \) and \( E\left( {b, a}\right) \) are independent of one another. We can define the class of undirected graphs to be graphs where the edge relation is symmetric, that is, the directed graphs satisfying \( \forall x, y\left( {E\left( {x, y}\right)  \rightarrow  E\left( {y, x}\right) }\right) \) .

- The usual axioms for groups, rings, and fields define the class of groups, rings, and fields, respectively.

For examples of definitions in the second sense, fix the structure \( \mathfrak{N} \) to be \( \left( {\mathbb{N}, + }\right) \) . Then the predicate "is equal to 0" on the universe of that structure is defined by the formula \( {A}_{0}\left( x\right)  \equiv  x + x = x \) , the relation "is less than or equal to" is defined by the formula \( {A}_{ \leq  }\left( {x, y}\right)  \equiv \; \exists z\left( {x + z = y}\right) \) , and the predicate "is even" is defined by the formula \( {A}_{\text{ even }}\left( x\right)  \equiv  \exists y\left( {y + y = x}\right) \) . None of these definitions require parameters from the model, which is to say, the definitions don't mention any elements of the universe of the particular model in which the definition is interpreted. For an example that does, consider the set of nonnegative real numbers in the structure \( \left( {\mathbb{R}, \leq  }\right) \) . We will see below that this set is not definable in the language with \( \leq \) alone, but it clearly is definable by the formula \( 0 \leq  x \) if we are allowed to refer to 0 .

Even when we deal with formal derivations, we generally take ourselves to be proving things about objects, functions, and relations in structures that are of interest to us. As a result, it is important to know what sorts of things can be expressed in the languages we have at our disposal. This is one way in which the theory of first-order definability is helpful.

Showing that a class of structures is definable is usually straightforward: one describes a sentence, or a set of sentences, defining it. Showing that a class of structures is not definable generally requires more ingenuity. Recall the notion of isomorphism from Section 1.1. An important observation is that first-order logic cannot distinguish isomorphic structures.

Theorem 5.5.1. Suppose \( \varphi \) is an isomorphism between two structures \( \mathfrak{A} \) and \( \mathfrak{B} \) , \( A\left( {{x}_{0},\ldots ,{x}_{n - 1}}\right) \) is a formula with the free variables shown, and \( {a}_{0},\ldots ,{a}_{n - 1} \) are elements of \( \left| \mathfrak{A}\right| \) . Then

\[
\mathfrak{A} \vDash  A\left( {{a}_{0},\ldots ,{a}_{n - 1}}\right) \;\text{ if and only if }\mathfrak{B} \vDash  A\left( {\varphi \left( {a}_{0}\right) ,\ldots ,\varphi \left( {a}_{n - 1}\right) }\right) \text{ . }
\]

In particular, isomorphic structures satisfy exactly the same sentences. The proof is a straightforward induction on formulas, using the defining clauses for the satisfaction relation.

The compactness theorem is a useful tool. As was the case with propositional logic, it can be stated in two forms.

Theorem 5.5.2. Let \( \Gamma \) be any set of sentences.

- If every finite subset of \( \Gamma \) has a model, then \( \Gamma \) has a model.

- If \( \Gamma  \vDash  A \) for some sentence \( A \) , then for some finite \( {\Gamma }^{\prime } \subseteq  \Gamma ,{\Gamma }^{\prime } \vDash  A \) .

As for propositional logic, these are easily shown to be equivalent, and both follow immediately from soundness and completeness.

Theorem 5.5.3. Let \( \Gamma \) be any set of first-order sentences with arbitrarily large finite models. Then \( \Gamma \) has an infinite model.

Proof Let \( {A}_{n} \) be a first-order sentence that holds in a model if and only if the model has at least \( n \) elements. The hypothesis implies that every finite subset of \( \Gamma  \cup  \left\{  {{A}_{0},{A}_{1},{A}_{2},\ldots }\right\} \) has a model. By compactness, the whole set has a model,

Corollary 5.5.4. The class of finite sets cannot be defined by any set \( \Gamma \) of first-order sentences.

Proof Suppose the class of finite sets is defined by the set \( \Gamma \) . For each \( n \) , let \( {A}_{n} \) be the sentence that says that there are at least \( n \) elements of the universe. Then every finite subset of \( \Gamma  \cup  \left\{  {{A}_{0},{A}_{1},{A}_{2},\ldots }\right\} \) has a model but the full set does not, contrary to the compactness theorem.

Corollary 5.5.5. The class of infinite sets cannot be defined by any finite set of first-order formulas.

Proof Suppose the class were defined by the finite set \( \Gamma \) . Then it would be defined by the single sentence \( A \equiv  \bigwedge \Gamma \) , in which case \( \neg A \) would define the class of finite sets, contrary to Corollary 5.5.4.

A binary relation \( R \) on a set \( A \) is said to be well founded if every nonempty subset \( S \subseteq  A \) has an \( R \) -minimal element; in other words, if for every nonempty \( S \) there is an \( x \) in \( S \) such that for no \( z \) in \( S \) do we have \( R\left( {z, x}\right) \) . Exercise 5.5.2 below asks you to show that this is equivalent to saying that there is no infinite sequence of elements \( {x}_{0},{x}_{1},{x}_{2},\ldots \) such that for every \( i, R\left( {{x}_{i + 1},{x}_{i}}\right) \) . (See also Appendix A.2.)

Theorem 5.5.6. The class of well-founded relations is not first-order definable.

Proof Suppose \( \Gamma \) defines the class of well-founded relations in a language with a single binary relation symbol, \( R \) . Add countably many constants \( {c}_{0},{c}_{1},{c}_{2},\ldots \) and consider the set of sentences \( \Delta  = \Gamma  \cup  \left\{  {R\left( {{c}_{i + 1},{c}_{i}}\right) }\right\} \) . Let \( {\Delta }^{\prime } \) be any finite subset of \( \Delta \) , and suppose \( n \) is the largest number such that the sentence \( R\left( {{c}_{n + 1},{c}_{n}}\right) \) appears in \( \Delta \) . Then we obtain a model of \( {\Delta }^{\prime } \) with universe \( \{ 0,1,\ldots , n + 1\} \) by taking \( R \) to be \( < \) and interpreting \( {c}_{0} \) to be \( n + 1,{c}_{1} \) to be \( n \) , and so on. (It doesn’t matter what we assign to \( {c}_{j} \) when \( j > n + 1 \) .) By compactness, \( \Delta \) has a model, \( \mathfrak{M} \) . But in that model, we have \( {R}^{\mathfrak{M}}\left( {{c}_{i + 1}^{\mathfrak{M}},{c}_{i}^{\mathfrak{M}}}\right) \) for every \( i \) , contrary to our initial assumption and the fact that \( \mathfrak{M} \) is a model of \( \Gamma \) .

As far as the second sort of definability, one way to show that a relation is not definable in a structure (without parameters) is to show that there is an automorphism of the structure that is not compatible with the relation. For example:

Theorem 5.5.7. The relation \( < \) is not definable in \( \mathfrak{R} = \left( {\mathbb{R},0, + }\right) \) .

Proof The function \( f\left( x\right)  =  - x \) is an automorphism of the structure. Suppose \( A\left( {x, y}\right) \) defines the less-than relation. Then we have \( \mathfrak{R} \vDash  A\left( {1,2}\right) \) . By Theorem 5.5.1, we then have \( \mathfrak{R} \vDash  A\left( {-1, - 2}\right) \) , contradicting the fact that \( - 1 \nless   - 2 \) .

Proving interesting undefinability results generally requires more work than that. Exercise 5.5.10 offers an example where compactness together with an automorphism does the trick. Another powerful body of methods are based on what are called Ehrenfeucht-Fraissé games. We can sometimes obtain undefinability results by bringing computability issues into play. For example, it is impossible define multiplication in \( \left( {\mathbb{N}, + }\right) \) , because the theory of this structure is decidable whereas the theory of \( \left( {\mathbb{N},+, \cdot  }\right) \) is not. (See Section 12.5.)

One can ask whether it is possible to define a class consisting of a single structure using a set of sentences in first-order logic. Given a set of sentences, \( \Gamma \) , let \( \operatorname{Mod}\left( \Gamma \right) \) denote the class of its models. In the other direction, given a structure, \( \mathfrak{M} \) , let \( \operatorname{Th}\left( \mathfrak{M}\right) \) be the theory of \( \mathfrak{M} \) , that is, the set of all sentences true of \( \mathfrak{M} \) . What we are asking is whether there is a \( \Gamma \) such that \( \operatorname{Mod}\left( \Gamma \right) \) consists of a single element, or, equivalently, whether there is a model \( \mathfrak{M} \) such that \( \operatorname{Th}\left( \mathfrak{M}\right) \) has no other models.

A moment's reflection shows that this is too much to ask for: by Theorem 5.5.1, the best we can do is define a structure up to isomorphism, i.e. define the class of structures isomorphic to the one we are after. A set of sentences \( \Gamma \) such that all the elements of \( \operatorname{Mod}\left( \Gamma \right) \) are isomorphic is said to be categorical. We will see in the next section that even that is too ambitious: a theory is categorical if and only if it is the theory of a finite structure. If \( \lambda \) is a cardinal number, a set of sentences \( \Gamma \) is said to be \( \lambda \) -categorical if all its models of cardinality \( \lambda \) are isomorphic. Indeed, some interesting structures have \( \lambda \) -categorical theories. We will show, however, that the theory of the natural numbers is not \( {\aleph }_{0} \) -categorical, which is to say, there are countable models of the theory of the natural numbers that are not isomorphic to the standard interpretation. (In fact, the theory is not \( \lambda \) -categorical for any \( \lambda \) .)

Two structures \( \mathfrak{M} \) and \( \mathfrak{N} \) for a language \( L \) are said to be elementarily equivalent if \( \operatorname{Th}\left( \mathfrak{M}\right)  = \operatorname{Th}\left( \mathfrak{N}\right) \) . This means that \( \mathfrak{M} \) and \( \mathfrak{N} \) satisfy exactly the same first-order sentences, and hence are indistinguishable, as far as first-order logic is concerned. By Theorem 5.5.1, if \( \mathfrak{M} \) and \( \mathfrak{N} \) are isomorphic then they are elementarily equivalent, but the converse does not hold. For example, we will see in Section 11.2 that the structure \( \left( {\mathbb{Q}, < }\right) \) is elementarily equivalent to \( \left( {\mathbb{R}, < }\right) \) , but the two structures are not isomorphic, since \( \mathbb{Q} \) is countable but \( \mathbb{R} \) isn't.

Theorem 5.5.8. Let \( \mathfrak{N} \) be the structure \( \left( {\mathbb{N},0\text{ , succ, }+,\cdot , < }\right) \) . There is a countable structure \( \mathfrak{M} \) such that

- \( \mathfrak{M} \) is elementarily equivalent to \( \mathfrak{N} \) , and

- \( \mathfrak{M} \) is not isomorphic to \( \mathfrak{N} \) .

Proof Let \( L \) be the language of \( \mathfrak{N} \) , and \( {L}^{\prime } \) be \( L \) together with a new constant \( c \) . Let \( \Gamma \) be the following set of sentences in \( {L}^{\prime } \) :

\[
\operatorname{Th}\left( \mathfrak{N}\right)  \cup  \{ 0 < c,\operatorname{succ}\left( 0\right)  < c,\operatorname{succ}\left( {\operatorname{succ}\left( 0\right) }\right)  < c,\ldots \} .
\]

Every finite subset \( {\Gamma }^{\prime } \) of \( \Gamma \) has a model of the form \( \left( {\mathbb{N},0, S,+,\cdot , < , m}\right) \) , where \( m \) is a natural number that is large enough to satisfy the finitely many sentences involving \( c \) in \( {\Gamma }^{\prime } \) . By compactness, \( \Gamma \) has a model. Let \( \mathfrak{M} \) be the result of restricting this structure to the language \( L \) . We have \( \mathfrak{M} \vDash  \operatorname{Th}\left( \mathfrak{N}\right) \) since \( \operatorname{Th}\left( \mathfrak{N}\right) \) is a subset of \( {\Gamma }^{\prime } \) ., and so \( \mathfrak{M} \) is elementarily equivalent to \( \mathfrak{N} \) . On the other hand, by construction, there is an element \( a \) in the universe of \( \mathfrak{M} \) that is bigger than \( {0}^{\mathfrak{M}} \) , succ \( {\left( 0\right) }^{\mathfrak{M}} \) , succ(succ(0)) \( {}^{\mathfrak{M}} \) , \( \ldots \) . This implies that \( \mathfrak{M} \) is not isomorphic to \( \mathfrak{N} \) .

The elements \( {0}^{\mathfrak{M}} \) , succ \( {\left( 0\right) }^{\mathfrak{M}} \) , succ(succ(0)) \( {}^{\mathfrak{M}} \) , \( \ldots \) of such a nonstandard model \( \mathfrak{M} \) of arithmetic are called the standard elements of \( \mathfrak{M} \) , and the remaining elements of the universe are called the nonstandard elements. In a sense, a nonstandard number is infinitely large. But only in a sense; as far as \( \mathfrak{M} \) is concerned, such an element is a natural number just like any other.

What does a nonstandard model of arithmetic look like? All of the following sentences are true of the natural numbers, and hence true in any such model.

1. \( < \) is a linear order.

2. Every element has a unique successor.

3. Every element other than 0 has a unique predecessor.

4. Every element is even or odd.

5. For every \( x \) and \( y \) , if \( x > y \) then \( x + x > x + y \) .

6. If \( x \) is even, then there is a number \( y \) such that \( y + y = x \) .

7. There are infinitely many primes.

8. Fermat's last theorem holds.

If we draw a picture, we see that every nonstandard number is a member of a countable chain that is order-isomorphic to the integers, since it has infinitely many predecessors and infinitely many successors. Statement 5 can be used to show that there are infinitely many such chains, and statement 6 can be used to show that between any two such chains, there is another one. (See Exercise 5.5.13.)

The same trick can be used to construct nonstandard models of any theory of the real numbers, in which there are positive real numbers that are smaller than \( 1,1/2,1/3,1/4,\ldots \) Such numbers can be considered infinitely small, and, interestingly enough, can be used to make some sense of the informal understanding of infinitesimals in the historical development of calculus. Using nonstandard models to justify reasoning about infinitesimals forms the basis of a field known as nonstandard analysis.

### 5.6 Some Model Theory

This section introduces some additional concepts and results that are fundamental to the model theory of classical first-order logic.

Let \( {L}_{1} \) and \( {L}_{2} \) be languages and suppose \( {L}_{1} \) is a subset of \( {L}_{2} \) , in the sense that every function, relation, and constant symbol of \( {L}_{1} \) is also a symbol of \( {L}_{2} \) . Given an \( {L}_{1} \) -structure \( \mathfrak{A} \) , it is often useful to consider an expansion of \( \mathfrak{A} \) to an \( {L}_{2} \) -structure \( \mathfrak{B} \) . This means that \( \mathfrak{A} \) and \( \mathfrak{B} \) have the same universe and interpret the symbols of \( {L}_{1} \) the same way, so \( \mathfrak{B} \) only adds interpretations of the additional symbols of \( {L}_{2} \) . If \( \mathfrak{B} \) is an expansion of \( \mathfrak{A} \) , then \( \mathfrak{A} \) is said to be a reduct of \( \mathfrak{B} \) . For example, \( \left( {\mathbb{N},0, < }\right) \) is a reduct of \( \left( {\mathbb{N},0,1,+,\cdot , < }\right) \) for suitable \( {L}_{1} \) and \( {L}_{2} \) .

If \( \mathfrak{A} \) and \( \mathfrak{B} \) are structures for the same language, \( \mathfrak{A} \) is a substructure of \( \mathfrak{B} \) , written \( \mathfrak{A} \subseteq  \mathfrak{B} \) , if the universe of \( \mathfrak{A} \) is a subset of the universe of \( \mathfrak{B} \) , the functions of \( \mathfrak{A} \) are the restrictions of the functions of \( \mathfrak{B} \) to the universe of \( \mathfrak{A} \) , and the relations of \( \mathfrak{A} \) are just the restrictions of the relations of \( \mathfrak{B} \) to the universe of \( \mathfrak{A} \) . For example, \( \left( {\mathbb{N},0,+, \cdot  }\right) \) is a substructure of \( \left( {\mathbb{R},0,+, \cdot  }\right) \) , but \( \left( {\mathbb{Q}, < }\right) \) is not a substructure of \( \left( {\mathbb{R}, > }\right) \) .

\( \mathfrak{A} \) is an elementary substructure of \( \mathfrak{B} \) if it is a substructure of \( \mathfrak{B} \) and the following holds: whenever \( {a}_{0},\ldots ,{a}_{k - 1} \) are elements of the universe of \( \mathfrak{A} \) and \( A\left( {{x}_{0},\ldots ,{x}_{k - 1}}\right) \) is a formula, then \( \mathfrak{A} \vDash  A\left( {{a}_{0},\ldots ,{a}_{k - 1}}\right) \) if and only if \( \mathfrak{B} \vDash  A\left( {{a}_{0},\ldots ,{a}_{k - 1}}\right) \) . This says more than that \( \mathfrak{A} \) is a substructure of \( \mathfrak{B} \) and that \( \mathfrak{A} \) is elementarily equivalent to \( \mathfrak{B} \) , since \( \mathfrak{A} \) and \( \mathfrak{B} \) have to agree not just on the truth of sentences in the underlying language, but on sentences with parameters from the universe of \( \mathfrak{A} \) as well. For example, \( \left( {{\mathbb{N}}^{ > 0}, < }\right) \) is a substructure of \( \left( {\mathbb{N}, < }\right) \) , and since the two structures are isomorphic, they are elementarily equivalent. But in the first structure, the sentence \( \exists x\left( {x < y}\right) \) is false when \( y \) is 1, whereas it is true in the second. The following, known as the Tarski-Vaught test, gives a necessary and sufficient condition for one structure to be an elementary substructure of another.

Proposition 5.6.1. Suppose \( \mathfrak{A} \) and \( \mathfrak{B} \) are structures for a language \( L \) , and \( \mathfrak{A} \) is a substructure of \( \mathfrak{B} \) . Then \( \mathfrak{A} \) is an elementary substructure of \( \mathfrak{B} \) if and only if the following holds: whenever \( A\left( {\overrightarrow{x}, y}\right) \) is any formula in \( L,\overrightarrow{c} \) is any tuple of elements from \( \left| \mathfrak{A}\right| \) , and \( b \) is an element of \( \left| \mathfrak{B}\right| \) such that \( \mathfrak{B} \vDash  A\left( {\overrightarrow{c}, b}\right) \) , then there is an element a of \( \left| \mathfrak{A}\right| \) such that \( \mathfrak{B} \vDash  A\left( {\overrightarrow{c}, a}\right) \) .

The fact that the condition holds when \( \mathfrak{A} \) is an elementary substructure of \( \mathfrak{B} \) is immediate. The other direction can be proved using induction on formulas, and the details are left as an exercise.

If \( \varphi \) is an embedding of \( \mathfrak{A} \) into \( \mathfrak{B} \) , then \( \varphi \) is also an isomorphism of \( \mathfrak{A} \) with its image in \( \mathfrak{B} \) , interpreted as a substructure of \( \mathfrak{B} \) . It is called an elementary embedding if the image of \( \mathfrak{A} \) is an elementary substructure of \( \mathfrak{B} \) .

It is not hard to check that the nonstandard model of arithmetic asserted to exist by Theorem 5.5.8 is an elementary extension of a structure that is isomorphic to the standard model, \( \mathfrak{N} \) . This is because the language of arithmetic has names 0, succ(0), succ(succ(0)),… for each natural number and the theory specifies the behavior of the function and relation symbols on those terms. In general, it is not the case that every element of a structure is denoted by some closed term in the underlying language. But if \( \mathfrak{A} \) is an \( L \) -structure, we can always add a new constant \( \bar{a} \) for each element \( a \) of \( \mathfrak{A} \) to obtain the language \( L\left( \mathfrak{A}\right) \) . The diagram of \( \mathfrak{A} \) is defined to be the set of atomic and negated atomic sentences of \( L\left( \mathfrak{A}\right) \) that are true in \( \mathfrak{A} \) . The complete diagram of \( \mathfrak{A} \) is defined to be the set of all the first-order sentences of \( L\left( \mathfrak{A}\right) \) that are true in \( \mathfrak{A} \) .

Proposition 5.6.2. \( \mathfrak{A} \) is isomorphic to a substructure of \( \mathfrak{B} \) if and only if \( \mathfrak{B} \) can be expanded to a model of the diagram of \( \mathfrak{A} \) . \( \mathfrak{A} \) is isomorphic to an elementary substructure of \( \mathfrak{B} \) if and only if \( \mathfrak{B} \) can be expanded to a model of the complete diagram of \( \mathfrak{A} \) .

Proof If \( \varphi  : \left| \mathfrak{A}\right|  \rightarrow  \left| \mathfrak{B}\right| \) is an isomorphism, then \( \mathfrak{B} \) can be expanded to a model of the diagram of \( \mathfrak{A} \) by interpreting each constant \( \bar{a} \) by \( \varphi \left( a\right) \) . Conversely, if \( \mathfrak{B} \) is a model of the diagram of \( \mathfrak{A} \) , the map \( \varphi  : a \mapsto  {\bar{a}}^{\mathfrak{B}} \) is an isomorphism. The proof of the second part of the proposition is similar.
